Finding Your People

Loneliness isn’t a flaw in you. It’s a sign you haven’t found them yet.

Loneliness is the quiet epidemic of life abroad — the part people are most ashamed of and least likely to say out loud. So let’s say it: making friends as an adult in a new country is genuinely hard, and struggling with it has nothing to do with how likeable you are. This month, we’ll look at why it’s so hard, and the small, repeatable moves that actually grow connection.

What’s actually happening

Adult friendship is hard. Abroad, it’s on hard mode.

As kids, friendship was almost automatic — the same faces, every day, for years. That’s the secret recipe, and adulthood quietly removes it. Move abroad and it gets harder still: there’s a language between you and people, the rules for how friendship works are different, populations are transient (people you bond with leave), and you’re starting from zero with no shared history to stand on.

Loneliness isn’t evidence that something’s wrong with you. It’s evidence that you’re human, and you haven’t found your people yet.

It’s worth separating two things that get confused. Contact is a calendar full of acquaintances, small talk, polite coffees. Connection is being known. You can have a lot of the first and still feel achingly lonely — because the goal was never more contacts. It’s a few real ones.

And real connection forms in a predictable way: repetition + a little vulnerability + shared context. Which means the move isn’t to “be more outgoing” — it’s to put yourself in the same room as the same people, more than once, and let a little realness in.

You don’t need a big circle. You need a few people who get you — and those almost always come from showing up to the same place again and again.

The exercise

From contact to connection.

Connection is built, not stumbled into. Let’s find the repetition you already have, and one small step toward realness.

Where do you already have repeated contact that could deepen? (A class, a coworker, a neighbor, a barista, a school-gate parent.)
Who have you met that you’d actually like to know better — and what’s one small step toward it? (A coffee, a message, an honest question.)
Name one repeatable situation you’ll put yourself in this month where your people might be. (A club, class, group, run, volunteering, a regular spot.)
The grounding practice

For the Lonely Nights

For when tonight is one of the hard ones. We won’t fix the loneliness — we’ll just make sure you’re not alone in it.

1
Soften — Let your shoulders drop. One long breath out.
2
Meet it kindly — Loneliness hurts because you were built for connection. It’s a signal, not a flaw.
3
Your own warm hand — Hand over your heart, or around the other arm. Your body responds to your own touch the way it would to a friend’s.
4
The invisible company — Right now, all over the world, others are lying awake feeling exactly this — far from home, missing their people. You’re part of a quiet company tonight.
5
Stay with yourself — Softly: “This is hard, I’m not the only one, and I won’t abandon myself.”
