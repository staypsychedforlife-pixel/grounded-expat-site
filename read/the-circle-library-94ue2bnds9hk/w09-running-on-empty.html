Running on Empty

You’re not lazy. You’re running a hidden engine, all day, every day.

Some months abroad, the tiredness doesn’t make sense. You haven’t done anything “big,” yet you’re wiped, irritable, dreading small tasks. This isn’t you being weak or ungrateful — it’s a specific, well-documented thing, and this month we’ll name it and learn to refuel before you hit empty.

What’s actually happening

Adaptation fatigue: the tank you can’t see.

Living abroad runs a hidden engine all the time. Every unfamiliar moment — a language you’re translating in your head, a system you’re decoding, an unwritten rule you’re guessing at, the simple fact that nothing is automatic — quietly draws from a tank you can’t see. It’s called adaptation fatigue, and you can run it to empty without ever noticing the gauge.

It’s invisible for two reasons. Each thing feels too small to count (“it’s just buying stamps”), so you never tally them — but they add up all day. And you tell yourself you should be coping by now, so you push through the warning lights.

Adaptation fatigue isn’t weakness or laziness. It’s the predictable result of asking your brain to work overtime, every single day, with no autopilot.

The signs the tank is near empty: exhaustion that sleep doesn’t fix, a short fuse, dread of small errands, numbness, getting sick more, sudden cynicism about the place, wanting to hide. And here’s the reframe that changes everything: rest and boundaries aren’t rewards you earn by emptying the tank — they’re the maintenance that keeps the engine running. Saying no, taking a low-demand day, a day in your own language — that’s refuelling, not failing.

You can’t adapt your way out of adaptation fatigue. The only fix is to put some back in the tank — on purpose, before it’s empty.

The grounding practice

The Downshift

When the tank’s empty and everything’s too much, a long exhale is the body’s own off-switch. This signals safety to slow down.

1
Be held — Sit or lie down. Let whatever’s under you take your weight.
2
In for four — Breathe in gently for a count of four.
3
Out for six — Breathe out slowly for a count of six. The long out-breath is the part that downshifts you.
4
Repeat — A few more rounds, at your own pace, making the exhale the longest, softest part.
5
Let it be heavy — “Resting isn’t quitting. It’s how I keep going.” The list will wait. You matter more than it does.
