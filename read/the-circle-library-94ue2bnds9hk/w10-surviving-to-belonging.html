From Surviving to Belonging

Functioning isn’t the same as living. You’re allowed to want a real life here.

There’s a quiet turning point in living abroad that no one announces: the day surviving stops being enough. You’ve got the basics handled — you can shop, commute, function — but functioning isn’t living, and you start to want an actual life here, not just an existence. This month is about that shift, and how to build it on purpose.

What’s actually happening

Surviving gets you through. Belonging weaves you in.

For a long while, the whole job is survival — figure out the systems, get through the days, keep your head above water. That’s necessary, and it’s enough, until one day it isn’t. Wanting more than survival isn’t greedy, and it isn’t disloyal to the home you left. It’s the next, healthy stage.

Here’s the thing most people get wrong: they wait to feel like they belong, as if the place will eventually hand it over. It won’t. Belonging is built — out of three things: rituals (the small recurring moments that become “yours” — the Saturday market, the café that knows your order), roots (commitments that tie you in — a class, a project, a community, a longer lease, a plant you have to keep alive), and meaning (mattering to someone, contributing, having a purpose here).

Belonging isn’t something a place hands you. It’s something you build — one ritual, one root, one bit of meaning at a time.

And your brain won’t help by default — its negativity bias keeps scanning for what’s foreign and wrong. So belonging also takes a deliberate turn of attention: noticing and claiming what’s already good, already yours.

The grounding practice

Savoring Here

Your brain scans for what’s wrong — useful for survival, brutal for joy. This practices the opposite: deliberately letting something good in.

1
Breathe — One easy breath, settling in.
2
Find one good thing — Something about your life here you’d genuinely miss if it vanished. A view, a food, a sound, a small kindness, the light.
3
Hold it for ten seconds — Don’t rush past it. Letting it linger is how the brain turns a moment into a memory.
4
Notice the shift — Somewhere along the way, here started giving you things, not only taking them.
5
Let it be true — “I’m not only surviving this place anymore. I’m beginning to belong to it.”
