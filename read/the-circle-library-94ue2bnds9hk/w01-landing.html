Landing: Steadying the Overwhelm

Your first job abroad isn’t to thrive. It’s to catch your breath.

Welcome to your first month in The Circle. This workshop is short on purpose — when everything feels like too much, the last thing you need is one more big thing to do. We’ll name what’s actually happening in your body, then build three small anchors to steady you.

What’s actually happening

Your nervous system isn’t broken — it’s working overtime.

When you move somewhere new, your brain loses all its shortcuts. The language, the streets, the social rules, the supermarket layout — none of it is automatic yet, so your nervous system can’t relax into “I know this.” It stays on quiet alert, scanning. That’s exhausting, and it happens even when the move is something you chose and wanted. Good change is still stress to the body.

Feeling overwhelmed in the first weeks isn’t a sign you’ve made a mistake. It’s the predictable cost of asking your brain to rebuild its whole map at once.

Here’s a simple way to picture it. Imagine you have a window — inside it, you can think clearly, feel your feelings, and cope. When the “too much” piles up, you get pushed outside the window, in one of two directions:

• Revved up — anxious, irritable, can’t switch off, can’t sleep, tears close to the surface.
• Shut down — numb, flat, foggy, exhausted, wanting to hide under the covers.

Both are normal responses to overload — not proof you’re “not cut out for this.” And the way back inside the window isn’t a big breakthrough. It’s anchors: small, repeatable points of predictability that tell your body you’re safe enough here. That’s the whole work of this first month.

Notice where you are

Before we build, let’s be honest about right now.

What has felt like the most — the thing that tips you into “too much”?
When you’re overwhelmed, where do you feel it in your body?Tight chest, knotted stomach, heavy limbs, a buzzing you can’t settle…
When you go “outside your window,” which way do you tend to go — revved up, or shut down? (Both are okay to name.)
Name one thing — however small — that’s actually been okay, or even good, since you arrived.
The exercise

Your first three anchors.

You don’t need your whole life here figured out. You need three reliable points your nervous system can return to. Let’s name yours — keep them small enough that they’re almost too easy.

1

A daily rhythm

One small thing you’ll do at the same time each day — morning coffee by the window, a walk after lunch, the same wind-down before bed.

2

A place

One spot that’s starting to feel like yours — a café, a park bench, a corner of your home, a route you walk.

3

A person

One person you can be a little real with — here or back home. Connection is an anchor too, even from a distance.

The grounding practice

The Arrival Reset

A 3-minute reset for when the overwhelm spikes. You can do it anywhere — you don’t need quiet or privacy. The point isn’t to feel calm; it’s to remind your body where it is.

1
Plant & settle (30 sec) — Feel your feet on the floor and your weight in the chair. Let your out-breath get a little longer than your in-breath, three or four times.
2
Orient (1 min) — Look slowly around you. Name — in your head or out loud — 5 things you see, 4 you can touch, 3 you can hear, 2 you can smell, 1 you can taste. This tells your brain: I’m here, and here is safe enough.
3
Touch an anchor (1 min) — Bring one of your three anchors to mind. Picture the very next time you’ll have it.
4
Close (30 sec) — One hand on your chest. Say, quietly: “This is hard, and I’m doing it.”

🎧 An audio version of the Arrival Reset lives in The Circle library — press play on the overwhelmed days when reading is too much.

Take it to the circle

Pick one. Keep it daily.

Not three — one. Choose a single anchor from above and keep it every day this week, same small thing, same time. Repetition is what actually widens the window; your nervous system trusts what it can predict.

The one anchor I’ll keep daily this week:
Share in the community: one thing that’s felt overwhelming this week, and one tiny win.

You don’t have to love it here yet. You just have to give yourself enough small certainties to catch your breath. That’s the whole job this month — and you’re already doing it.
