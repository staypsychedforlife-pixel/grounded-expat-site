Far From Home for the Holidays

You don’t have to choose between missing home and being here.

Holidays abroad have a way of cracking you open. Most of the year you cope fine — and then a holiday arrives, with its smells and songs and the empty chair where your people should be, and the missing floods in all at once. This month is about meeting that ache honestly, and making the season something that fits the life you have now.

What’s actually happening

Holidays hit the exact spot a move disrupts.

Holidays are built out of tradition, ritual, togetherness, and memory — which is precisely what moving abroad scatters. So they land hard: the gap between how it’s “supposed” to be and how it actually is has never been wider, and every feed is full of other people’s gatherings. Of course it aches.

There are two traps. One is trying to recreate home exactly — which it can’t match, so you end up disappointed. The other is white-knuckling through, pretending you’re fine while something in you quietly breaks.

The goal isn’t to recreate home, and it isn’t to pretend you don’t miss it. It’s to honor what was — and build something that fits the life you have now.

The way through is both/and: you can grieve the holiday you’re missing and make new meaning here. Holding both is far healthier than forcing yourself to pick one. New traditions don’t betray the old ones — they can borrow from them, shrink them to fit, or be something entirely your own, shared with whoever your people are this year.

The grounding practice

Both / And

For the season that holds two true things at once. We don’t have to choose between them.

1
Settle — A slow breath out.
2
Let the missing in — Bring to mind what you’re missing this season — the people, the traditions, the place that knew you. Let it be tender. Don’t rush it.
3
Let what’s here in too — Without pushing the missing away, bring in what is here: the people, however few; a new tradition; the simple fact that you’re still here, still going.
4
Both hands — One hand open for the grief. The other open for the gratitude. “I miss home, AND I can make meaning here.”
5
Rest — You don’t have to pick one. The fullest hearts hold both. Let both hands rest.
