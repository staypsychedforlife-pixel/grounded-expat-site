Who Am I Here? Identity in Transition

You didn’t lose yourself in the move. You lost the scaffolding.

If you feel a little invisible here — like a flatter, smaller version of yourself, like no one quite knows who you are — this month is for you. The roles that used to tell you (and everyone else) who you were got left behind in the move, and rebuilding a self from scratch is disorienting work. Let’s name what happened, and find the part of you that came with you.

What’s actually happening

Your identity had scaffolding. The move took it down.

Who you are was never only inside you — it was held up by scaffolding. Your job, and the competence everyone could see. Your friendships, and the history behind them. Your community, your routines, the context that reflected you back to yourself a hundred times a day. Move countries, and that scaffolding comes down all at once.

Feeling unsure who you even are here — invisible, or like a watered-down version of yourself — isn’t a breakdown. It’s what happens when the mirrors that used to show you yourself are suddenly gone.

There’s a useful distinction underneath this. Roles are the labels — your title, “the funny one,” “the capable one,” someone’s partner, the person who always knew the best place to eat. They’re real, but they’re tied to a context, and some of them got left at the airport. Values are deeper — what you care about, how you want to move through the world, what you’d stand for anywhere. Roles don’t travel well. Values come with you.

You didn’t lose yourself. You lost the scaffolding. What’s underneath is still here — and you get to rebuild, on purpose, into who you actually want to be.

That’s the quiet gift buried in the disorientation: when the old labels fall away, you get a rare, clean chance to ask who you are underneath them — and who you’d like to become in this new place.

The exercise

Underneath the labels.

Let’s separate the roles you held from the self that holds them — and find the part of you that no border can touch.

Finish this a few times: “Back home, I was the one who ___.”
Now the truer question: who are you underneath those roles? What’s true about you in any country, with or without the labels?
Name one value you want this chapter — this version of you — to be built around.
The grounding practice

The Values Touchstone

When you don’t know who you are here yet, it helps to remember what you’re for. This finds you solid ground in about two minutes.

1
Settle — One slow breath out.
2
Find the moment — Recall a time you felt most like yourself. Not your most impressive moment — your most you moment.
3
Look closer — What were you doing? Who were you being?
4
Name the value — Underneath that moment is something you care about at the core. Courage. Care. Curiosity. Honesty. See what word rises.
5
Claim it — That value came with you. Your title may not have. Your community may not have. But that did. Hand somewhere steady: “I am still someone who values ___.”
