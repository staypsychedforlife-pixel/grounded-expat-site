The Dip: Culture Shock for Real

It gets harder before it gets easier. That’s the map — not a wrong turn.

Welcome back. If the shine has worn off — if things feel harder now than when you first arrived — you are not failing, and you did not choose wrong. You’ve hit the most predictable stage of moving abroad, the one almost no one warns you about.

What’s actually happening

Culture shock isn’t a bad day. It’s a curve.

Almost everyone who moves abroad moves through the same predictable stages. Knowing the shape of it changes everything — because when you can see you’re in a stage, you stop mistaking it for the truth about your whole life.

  1. The honeymoonEverything’s new and exciting. The differences feel like an adventure. (Often the first weeks to a couple of months.)
  2. The dipThe novelty wears off and daily friction piles up — the admin, the language, the loneliness, the missing home. It can feel worse than week one. This is usually months 3–9.
  3. The climbSlowly, competence and connection build. You start to find your footing. It begins to feel workable.
  4. At home enoughIt becomes a life, not a trial. Not perfect — but yours.

The dip isn’t a verdict on the place, or on you. It’s a stage — and stages, by definition, pass.

Here’s the cruel timing: the honeymoon’s adrenaline fades right as the accumulated fatigue peaks, and the “I’m new here” grace period runs out. So the hardest stretch tends to land exactly when you expected to feel settled. That mismatch is normal — not a sign something’s gone wrong.

And here’s the trap to watch: in the dip, your mind tells very convincing stories — “this place is wrong,” “everyone’s happier back home,” “I made a mistake.” The dip distorts. Simply knowing that is your superpower this month.

The exercise

The dip vs. what’s also true.

The dip talks loudly and sounds like fact. Let’s put its claims next to the fuller picture you can see on a steadier day. This isn’t forced positivity — it’s just refusing to let the dip have the only say.

When you’re deep in it, what does the dip tell you?Write the actual thoughts — e.g. “I made a mistake,” “it’ll never feel like home,” “everyone else has it figured out.”
Now, from a steadier place — what’s also true that the dip conveniently forgets?
Name one sign — however small — that you are adjusting. Something you handled this week that would’ve floored you in week one.
The grounding practice

Name It to Tame It

When the dip floods you, naming the feeling literally lowers its charge in the brain. You don’t have to fix it — just name it.

1
Settle — One slow breath, longer on the way out.
2
Turn inward — Gently, the way you’d check on a friend: what’s here right now?
3
One word — Not the whole story. Just a word: tired, flat, anxious, homesick, numb.
4
Locate it — Notice where it lives in your body. Chest, throat, stomach. Just find it.
5
Allow it — Say: “This is [the feeling], and it makes sense that it’s here.”
6
Remember — Feelings are weather, not climate. This one is moving through. It isn’t who you are, and it isn’t forever.
