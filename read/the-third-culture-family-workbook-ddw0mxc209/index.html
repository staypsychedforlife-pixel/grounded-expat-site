The Third-Culture Family Workbook

Helping your children — and yourself — feel at home abroad.
Created by Stephanie Johnson, LICSW

Moving abroad as a family carries a particular weight: you’re not only finding your own footing, you’re watching the people you love most try to find theirs. You worry whether you did the right thing. You hold your children’s grief on top of your own. And your kids are becoming ‘third-culture’ — shaped by more than one world, belonging fully to none in the usual way.

This workbook is for the parent’s heart and the family’s life together. It helps you tend your own ground, understand what the move asks of your children, and build the anchors that make a family feel at home anywhere. Part One

Your Own Ground First

Because they steady off you

You Can’t Pour From Empty

On a plane, they tell you to fix your own oxygen mask first — and it’s truer than ever abroad. Your children co-regulate off you; your steadiness is part of their safety. Tending your own adjustment isn’t selfish — it’s foundational.

A note for you

Taking care of your own footing isn’t taking something from your children. It’s giving them a steadier parent to come home to.

SFrom my work

In my work with families who’ve moved abroad, the parent’s own adjustment is the piece that quietly gets skipped — everyone’s watching the kids. But children read our nervous systems long before they hear our words. Again and again I’ve seen a child settle not because anything outside changed, but because their parent finally got some support and steadied. Tending your own footing isn’t a luxury for later; it’s part of the work.

— Stephanie, LICSW

What have you been carrying alone that you haven’t said out loud — to a partner, a friend, or even yourself?

The Guilt You Carry

Many expat parents carry a quiet guilt: Did I uproot them for my own reasons? Did I take away their friends, their grandparents, their sense of home? The guilt is a sign of how much you love them — but marinating in it doesn’t help them, and it isn’t the whole truth.

A note for you

You made a big decision for your family with the information and love you had. Guilt doesn’t make you a bad parent — but you don’t have to let it run the house.

What do you feel guilty about when it comes to how this move has affected your children? Name it specifically.
Reading back what you wrote — is that guilt pointing at something you could actually change or repair, or is it grief for something outside your control?
Then guilt is data, not a verdict — let’s turn it into one small action.
What’s the specific thing — a conversation, a change to the routine, more of something, less of something? Name it.
What’s the smallest version of that you could actually do this week? one small thing
Then this isn’t guilt to fix — it’s grief to feel. Naming it is how you stop carrying it as blame.
What is it you’re actually grieving on your children’s behalf — and can you let it be sad without it being your fault?
Now the other side: what did this move also GIVE them — experiences, growth, closeness, a wider world? Let yourself write it. exercise
Part Two

Your Children’s World

Understanding what the move asks of them

What the Move Asks of Your Kids

Children grieve moves too, just in their own language — through behavior, regression, clinginess, anger, or a too-quiet ‘I’m fine.’ Their sense of home, friendships, and routine were rearranged without their say. Understanding what they lost helps you meet what they show.

A note for you

Your child’s hard behavior after a move usually isn’t defiance — it’s grief and overwhelm without the words to say so. They’re not giving you a hard time; they’re having one.

For each of your children, what has this move asked them to leave behind — and how have you seen it show up in them?
Is there one child you’re most worried about right now?
Let’s slow down and really look at that one.
Which child, and what specifically are you noticing — in their behavior, mood, sleep, or the too-quiet ‘I’m fine’?
If their hard behavior is grief and overwhelm without the words, what might they be trying to tell you underneath it?
What’s one small way you could give this child a little more connection or predictability this week? one small thing
Then let’s scan gently across all of them.
For each child, write one line: how are they really doing — and what’s the one sign you’re going on?
Where have you seen any of them show resilience, or find something good here, even small?

The Third-Culture Identity

Kids raised across cultures grow up rich and rootless at once — fluent in more than one world, but sometimes unsure where ‘home’ or ‘from’ really is. This isn’t a problem to fix so much as an identity to support: they can belong to more than one place, and to your family most of all.

A note for you

Your child doesn’t have to choose one country to be ‘from.’ You can help them hold all of it — and know that home is also the people in it, not only a place on a map.

SFrom my work

One of the things I find myself reassuring expat parents about most is the ‘where are you from?’ question — the fear that their child will grow up rootless. What I’ve seen is often the opposite: children raised across cultures can grow up remarkably adaptable, empathetic, and at ease with difference. The rootedness they need isn’t a single country — it’s the steady sense of belonging to their family, wherever that family happens to be.

— Stephanie, LICSW

How does your child seem to answer ‘where are you from?’ — and what feelings come up around it for them (or for you)?
What parts of your home culture do you want to keep alive for them? What parts of this new culture do you hope they carry too?
How can you help them feel that being ‘from everywhere’ is a richness, not a rootlessness?
What’s one small ritual or object that could travel with your family as a constant ‘home,’ wherever you are? one small thing

Talking With Them About It

Children often can’t spontaneously explain how a move feels — but they can respond when we make it safe and ordinary to talk about. The goal isn’t a big serious conversation; it’s small, open doors, and a lot of listening without rushing to fix.

A note for you

You don’t need the perfect words. You need to be a safe place to bring the hard feelings — and to resist the urge to fix them away too quickly.

Have you tried to talk with them about the move — and did it open up, or shut down?
Good — let’s build on what’s already open.
What did they tell you — and what surprised you about how they’re really feeling?
When they share something hard, what’s your instinct — to fix, reassure, or minimize? What would just listening look like instead?
What’s one thing you could say that keeps that door open — that lets them know it’s safe to bring the hard stuff again? one small thing
That’s okay — the goal isn’t one big serious talk, just small, low-pressure openings.
When is each child most likely to open up — in the car, at bedtime, side by side doing something? Pick the moment.

Part Three

Your Family, Steadier

Building home wherever you land

Anchors, Rituals & Home

Home, for a mobile family, becomes less about an address and more about the constants you carry — the Friday pancakes, the bedtime song, the way you mark birthdays. Rituals tell a child’s nervous system: whatever changes outside, this family holds.

A note for you

You can’t give your children an unchanging place. You can give them an unchanging sense of ‘us’ — and that travels anywhere.

What rituals or traditions did your family have before the move that you want to protect? Which have slipped that you’d like back?
What new traditions could you build here that are unique to this chapter of your family’s life?
Make a short list of your family’s ‘anchors’ — the constants you’ll keep no matter where you live. exercise
