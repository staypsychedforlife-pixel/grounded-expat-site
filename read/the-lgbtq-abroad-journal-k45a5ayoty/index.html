The LGBTQ+ Abroad Journal

For the queer, trans, and questioning — the inner side of moving abroad as your whole self.
Created by Stephanie Johnson, LICSW

Moving abroad is its own upheaval. Doing it as an LGBTQ+ person adds layers most guides never mention: reading a new country for safety, deciding all over again who you can be open with, finding your people from scratch, and carrying an identity the place around you may celebrate, overlook, or forbid. Wherever you’ve landed on that spectrum, this journal is for the part of the move that’s specifically yours.

There’s no right way to be queer abroad, and no obligation to be out anywhere. This is a private space to think it through honestly — the safety, the exhaustion, the community, and the joy. Work through it in order or open the page you need. Your writing saves automatically on this device and stays private to you. Use Print / Save as PDF to keep a copy, or Download my writing to export it.

A note on privacy: your entries are stored only in this browser, on this device — never sent anywhere. If your situation makes privacy a matter of safety, you can clear them at any time, and it’s worth considering who else can access this device.

This journal is an educational, self-reflection resource — not therapy, diagnosis, treatment, or a crisis service. If you are somewhere that being LGBTQ+ is criminalized or unsafe, your physical safety comes first — nothing on this page is worth taking a risk for. If you’re struggling in a way that feels beyond what a journal can hold, please reach out to someone you trust, an affirming professional, or a local or international LGBTQ+ support line. You deserve real support, exactly as you are.
Part One

Landing as Your Whole Self

What this move asks of you

What This Move Means for You

Every LGBTQ+ move has a direction to it. Some people move somewhere freer and feel their shoulders drop; some move somewhere more restrictive and go quieter than they were; many land somewhere sideways — freer in one way, harder in another. Before anything else, let’s name what this particular move has meant for the part of you that’s queer, trans, or questioning.

A note for you

However this move has landed for your identity — relief, loss, complication, or all three — your read on it is valid. You’re allowed to feel the freedom and the cost at the very same time.

Compared to where you came from, is this new place more accepting of who you are — or less?
Then there may be a specific relief here — and sometimes an unexpected grief or disorientation that comes with new freedom. Let’s hold both.
What has loosened or become possible here that wasn’t, or didn’t feel safe, before? Let yourself notice it fully.
New freedom can feel strange after years of guarding. What’s surprised you — is there anything you’re still bracing for, or grieving from before?
What’s one small way you could let yourself actually receive this freedom this week — something you couldn’t do before? one small thing
Then this is a harder landing — and your caution isn’t paranoia, it’s wisdom. Let’s be honest about it, safely.
What parts of yourself have you had to put away, dial down, or hide since arriving? Name them here, where it’s yours.
What does that cost you day to day — the energy, the loneliness, the not-being-fully-known?
Where, or with whom, can you still be fully yourself here — even in small, private ways? If nowhere yet, what might a first pocket of that look like? one small thing

Reading the Room

Safety abroad isn’t paranoia — it’s information. Laws, everyday social attitudes, and specific places can differ wildly, and they’re not always what the guidebook says. This entry is about getting an honest, practical read on your actual environment, so your choices are informed rather than anxious guesses.

A note for you

Assessing your safety clearly isn’t living in fear. It’s what lets you relax where you can, and protect yourself where you must. Knowing is steadier than not-knowing.

What’s the real legal and social situation for LGBTQ+ people where you are — not the assumption, the actual picture? What do you still need to find out?
Which spaces, situations, or people here feel safe, which feel unsafe, and which are still a question mark?
Where might you be over- or under-estimating the risk — reacting to old fears, or to reassurance that isn’t quite true? What would help you actually know?
What’s one practical thing you could find out or set up this week — an affirming clinic, a legal fact, a trusted contact, a safer app setting? one small thing
Part Two

Out, Safe, and Whole

Identity & disclosure

Out, Not Out, or In Between

In a new country you often have to decide, all over again and person by person, who gets to know this about you. There’s no universally right answer — being out is not a virtue, and being private is not a failure. This is about making those choices consciously, on your own terms.

A note for you

You don’t owe anyone your disclosure — not here, not to prove anything. Being out, being selectively open, or being private are all legitimate. The only question that matters is what’s right and safe for you.

Right now, are you mostly able to be open about who you are here — or mostly navigating who’s safe to tell?
Then this is about deepening — being known, not just tolerated, and choosing where you invest.
Where do you feel genuinely known here, versus merely tolerated or unremarked-on? What’s the difference for you?
Is there a relationship or space where you could let yourself be a little more fully, joyfully out? What would that look like? one small thing
Then you’re doing constant, invisible calculation — and that’s exhausting, even when it’s the right call. Let’s make it conscious instead of automatic.
Who here knows, who doesn’t, and who are you unsure about? Getting it on paper can make the fog less anxious.
What does the constant reading-of-rooms and code-switching cost you? Where do you feel it most?
Where is there even one safe pocket — a person, an online space, a trip home — where you don’t have to calculate? How could you protect or grow it? one small thing

The Weight You Carry

Living as a minority — reading rooms, bracing for reactions, hiding or half-hiding — is a real, measurable load on the nervous system. It isn’t weakness or oversensitivity; it’s the cumulative cost of vigilance. Naming it is how you stop mistaking it for a flaw in you.

A note for you

The tiredness, the guardedness, the flashes of anger or numbness — these aren’t character defects. They’re the honest cost of navigating a world that isn’t always safe for who you are. You’re carrying something heavy, and it makes sense that it’s heavy.

SFrom my work

In my work, the piece that surprises people most is learning that this exhaustion has a name and a mechanism — minority stress. The constant, low-level vigilance of moving through a world that isn’t reliably safe for who you are is a genuine, measurable load on the nervous system, not a personal weakness. When someone finally hears that their tiredness makes sense, something loosens. You are not too sensitive; you’ve been carrying something real.

— Stephanie, LICSW

How does this particular load show up for you — hypervigilance, exhaustion, irritability, numbness, dread, or something else? Describe your version.
When is it heaviest — certain places, certain interactions, certain times? What tends to set it off?
What genuinely helps your system settle when it’s been on guard — affirming people, queer spaces, rest, distance from the source? List your real ones. body · grounding
What’s one thing you could do this week to be as kind to yourself as you’d be to a friend carrying this? one small thing
Part Three

Your People & Your Becoming

Community, love, and who you are

Finding Your People

Queer community can be one of the hardest things to rebuild abroad — sometimes hidden, sometimes small, sometimes online before it’s ever in person. And chosen family, that particular lifeline, has to be found again from scratch. This is about the brave, patient work of finding your people here.

A note for you

Needing queer community isn’t neediness — it’s oxygen. Being understood without having to explain yourself is something you deserve, and it’s worth the slow, brave work of building it here.

What kind of connection or community do you most miss — and what did it give you that you haven’t found here yet?
Where might your people be here — queer orgs, apps, events, online groups, affirming spaces, a friend of a friend? List every possibility, even faint ones. exercise
Who is your chosen family — near or far — and how will you keep those lifelines strong across the distance?
What’s one small, brave step toward queer connection you could take this month? one small thing

Love & Relationships Abroad

Love as an LGBTQ+ person abroad carries its own particular questions — where it’s safe to hold hands, whether your relationship is legally recognized, how to date in a new and maybe less-open place. This entry meets you wherever you are with it.

A note for you

Your love, your relationship, your desire for one — they’re real and they matter, whatever the law or the street says here. You get to want connection, and to protect it.

Are you navigating this partnered, or single (or dating)?
Then let’s tend the relationship in a context that may not make it easy.
What has this move asked of your relationship specifically — around safety, visibility, legal recognition, or the stress landing on you both?
Where can you be openly a couple here, and where can’t you? How does that shape your daily life together?
What’s one thing you could do to protect or nourish the relationship in this context this week? one small thing
Then let’s be honest about dating and desire here — the possibilities and the particular hurdles.
What do you actually want right now — connection, dating, community, just to feel less alone? Name it without judging it.
What makes dating or meeting people harder here specifically — a smaller scene, safety, the apps, the culture? What’s the honest picture?
What’s one low-pressure step toward the kind of connection you want — an app, an event, an online space — that feels safe enough to try? one small thing

Who You’re Becoming

For all its weight, being queer abroad also grows something in you — resilience, clarity about who your real people are, and often an unexpected freedom to redefine yourself far from the eyes that knew you. This last entry is for the becoming, and for the joy that’s yours to claim.

A note for you

You are not just surviving this — you’re becoming someone through it. The courage it takes to be yourself on unfamiliar ground is not small, and neither is the joy you’re allowed to claim along the way.

SFrom my work

Something I’ve witnessed again and again: the very people who’ve had to fight hardest to be themselves often develop a rare, hard-won steadiness — a clarity about who their real people are, and a refusal to abandon themselves that most people never have to build. The road here is unfair, and it is heavy. And still, the resilience and self-knowledge it can grow are genuinely yours to keep.

— Stephanie, LICSW

Who are you becoming through this experience — as a queer person, and as a whole person? What is it drawing out of you?
Has being far from home given you any unexpected freedom to be, explore, or redefine yourself? What have you discovered?
Where is the joy — the queer joy, the pride, the moments of being fully yourself — in this chapter? Let yourself name it, not only the hard parts.
What’s one thing about who you are, or who you’ve become here, that you want to carry forward no matter where you go next? one small thing

You get to be exactly who you are — here, and anywhere you go next. That’s yours to keep.
