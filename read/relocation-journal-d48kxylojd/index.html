The Relocation Journal

For the whole inner arc of moving abroad — from the landing to carrying it home.
Created by Stephanie Johnson, LICSW · The Grounded Expat

Moving abroad asks something of you that no checklist prepares you for — a whole inner journey that runs alongside the visas and the boxes. This journal walks that arc with you: the disorientation of landing, the hard middle where the shine wears off, the slow becoming, and the tender work of carrying it all home.

There are nine guided entries, grouped into four phases. You don’t have to do them in order, and you don’t have to do them all at once. Open the one that meets where you are today. Come back and answer again as you change — that’s the whole point.

Your writing saves automatically on this device and stays completely private to you. Use Print / Save as PDF to keep a copy, or Download my writing to export everything as a text file.

This is an educational, self-reflection resource — not therapy, diagnosis, or a crisis service. If you’re struggling beyond what journaling can hold, please reach out to someone you trust, a licensed professional, or local emergency services. You deserve real support.
Phase One

Landing

Entry One

Grounding in a New Place

The first task after a move isn’t to thrive — it’s simply to land. Before the mind can settle, the body needs to learn that it’s safe here. Start where your feet are.

Where are you writing from right now? Describe this new place through your senses — what you can see, hear, and smell around you.
What has felt hardest about this first stretch? Name it plainly, without softening it.
What’s one small thing — a corner, a café, a walk, a routine — that already feels the tiniest bit like yours?
Entry Two

Understanding Your Adjustment Arc

Culture shock isn’t a personal failing — it’s a predictable arc almost every mover travels: the honeymoon, the frustrated dip, the slow adjustment, and a hard-won at-home-ness. Seeing the map ahead of time makes the low points far less frightening.

Where do you think you are on that arc today — the shiny honeymoon, the frustrated middle, or somewhere in between?
What did you expect this move to feel like — and how does the reality compare?
If you truly believed the low points were normal and temporary, what would you stop being so hard on yourself about?
Entry Three

Your First Weeks

The first weeks ask a lot all at once — logistics and grief and newness, stacked on top of each other. This is a place to set some of that weight down.

What are you carrying right now — practical, emotional, all of it? Empty your pockets onto the page.
What’s one need you’ve been ignoring — rest, real food, a call home, a walk outside — that you could meet this week?
Who or what back home are you missing most today?
Phase Two

The Hard Middle

Entry Four

Culture Shock and the Nervous System

Culture shock isn’t only in your head — it’s in your body. A nervous system doing constant low-grade translation gets tired and touchy. That exhaustion is real, and it is not weakness.

How does the strain show up in your body — the short fuse, the deep fatigue, the tears that come from nowhere?
What small, ordinary things drain you here that were effortless back home?
What actually settles your system, even a little? List a few, so they’re ready the next time you need them.
Entry Five

Identity on the Move

A move can quietly unstitch the parts of you a place used to hold — your competence, your humour, your easy sense of who you are. Feeling like a stranger to yourself is one of the least-talked-about costs of leaving.

Who were you back home that you can’t quite be here — yet? What do you miss most about that version of yourself?
Where do you feel most incapable, or most “not yourself,” in your new life?
What part of you has survived the move fully intact — the thing that’s still unmistakably, stubbornly you?
Phase Three

Becoming

Entry Six

Belonging Abroad

Belonging abroad rarely arrives as a lightning bolt. It’s built slowly, in repeated small moments — a familiar face, a phrase you finally understand, a place that starts to greet you back.

When have you felt even a flicker of belonging here? What was happening in that moment?
What would this place need to feel like for it to feel like home — and what’s one small step toward that?
Where might you still be holding back from fully arriving — maybe to keep one foot in the old life?
Entry Seven

Who You’re Becoming

The move doesn’t only take — it remakes. Somewhere inside the difficulty, you’re becoming someone with a wider life and a bigger heart. It’s worth stopping to notice her.

What have you learned about yourself here that you couldn’t have learned anywhere else?
What are you more capable of now than the day you arrived?
If the version of you from before the move could see you now, what would surprise her most?
Phase Four

Carrying It Home

Entry Eight

Coming Home

Reverse culture shock is the one nobody warns you about: returning “home” to find that it — and you — no longer fit the way you did. The disorientation of coming back is real, and it deserves the same gentleness as the leaving.

If you’ve returned — or when you imagine it — what feels different? About home, and about you?
What did living abroad change in you that the people back home might not be able to see?
How do you want to hold onto what the move gave you, even back in familiar places?
Entry Nine

Year One — Your Legacy

A year on — wherever “here” is now — is a chance to look back across the whole arc and name what it made of you. Not the highlight reel; the real one.

Looking back across this move, what are you most proud of surviving?
What do you most want to remember about this chapter, years from now?
Write a few lines to someone about to make the leap you made. What do you most want them to know?

You carried a whole life across the world.

However far along the arc you are — landing, deep in the middle, or looking back — the fact that you’re here, doing this honest inner work, is the quiet proof of how much you’re capable of.

Come back to these pages whenever you need them. Your answers will change as you do, and that’s exactly right. Be gentle with yourself. 🌿
