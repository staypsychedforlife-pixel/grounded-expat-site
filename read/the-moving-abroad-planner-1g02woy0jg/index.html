The Moving Abroad Planner

The practical and emotional prep, in one place.
Created by Stephanie Johnson, LICSW

Most moving guides cover the logistics and stop there. But the part that decides how this actually goes — the inner preparation — usually gets none. This planner holds both: the checklists that get you out the door, and the quieter work that helps you arrive whole.

Tick the checklists as you go, write where it helps, and let the rest stop living in your head. Everything saves automatically on this device — your ticks and your writing — and stays private to you. Use Print / Save as PDF to keep a copy, or Download my plan to export it.

This planner is an educational, self-reflection resource — not therapy, diagnosis, treatment, or a crisis service. If you’re struggling in a way that feels beyond what a planner can hold, please reach out to someone you trust, a licensed professional, or your local emergency services. You deserve real support.
Part One

Before You Go

The weeks & months before

The Goodbye You Don’t Skip

The logistics of a move scream for attention; the emotional preparation gets almost none. But a move you’ve grieved a little in advance is one you arrive to more whole. Before the boxes, let’s tend to the leaving.

A note for you

Letting yourself feel the loss of what you’re leaving isn’t being negative about the move. It’s how you carry those people and places lightly, instead of being ambushed by them later.

What — and who — are you setting down with this move? Name the people, routines, and version of your life you’re leaving.
Is there a goodbye — to a person, a place, a chapter — you want to do properly before you leave? What would that look like?
What’s one small thing you’ll bring that carries home with you — an object, a recipe, a ritual? one small thing

The Practical Runway

Here’s the practical backbone — the admin that’s far less stressful handled early than in a panic the week before. Tick what’s done, note what’s pending, and let the rest stop living in your head.

A note for you

You don’t have to hold all of this in your head at once. That’s exactly what a list is for.

Pre-departure checklist tick as you go
What’s still outstanding — and what’s the next deadline you don’t want to miss?
Who can help you with the heaviest items — and what could you ask them for this week? one small thing

Setting Honest Expectations

The move itself may go smoothly — and then, somewhere in the first few months, things often get harder than people expect. Knowing that in advance takes away half its power. Let’s set expectations you can actually live with.

A note for you

You’re not going to feel at home right away. You’re not supposed to. Knowing the dip is coming is what keeps it from knocking you over.

What are you quietly most excited about — and most afraid of — about this move?
Realistically, what would ‘this is going well’ look like six months in — not the highlight reel, the honest version?
Name the one or two things you absolutely refuse to let this move cost you (your relationship, your health, a sense of who you are). one small thing
Is this your first big move abroad, or have you done this before?
Then set the bar gently — you don’t yet know what you don’t know, and that’s completely normal.
What are you assuming this will be like that you’re not actually sure about? Naming your assumptions now makes the surprises smaller later.
Who do you know who’s moved abroad that you could ask what they wish they’d known? Note one person to reach out to. one small thing
Then you have real data — let’s use it.
What did your last move teach you — what went harder than expected, and what actually helped?
What’s one thing you’ll do differently this time because of what you learned? one small thing

Your Arrival Kit

Arriving is easier when a few things are lined up in advance — the people you’ll stay close to, the routine you’ll try to recreate, the first small thing that will make the new place feel like yours.

A note for you

You don’t need it all figured out. You just need a few anchors waiting for you on the other side.

Who back home will you stay genuinely connected to — and how will you protect those few relationships across the distance?
What daily routine or ritual will you try to recreate in the new place, to give your system something familiar?
What’s the first small thing you’ll do on arrival to make the new place feel even slightly like yours? one small thing
Part Two

The First Weeks

Landing well on the other side

Landing Checklist

The first weeks are a blur of setup. Here’s a starting checklist — adapt it to your country. Done is better than perfect; the goal is to get the essentials handled so your nervous system can finally exhale.

A note for you

You won’t get it all sorted in week one, and that’s fine. Each small thing handled is one less open loop.

First-weeks checklist tick as you go
What still feels overwhelming or unfinished — and what’s the single next step on it?
What’s one social or community thing you’ll actually try in the next two weeks? one small thing

Your Support Plan

This is the one most people skip — and the one that matters most when a hard week comes. Set it up now, while you’re clear-headed: the simple architecture of who and what you’ll reach for.

A note for you

Planning for the hard days isn’t pessimism. It’s the same reason you’d find the exits before there’s ever a fire.

What are your earliest warning signs that you’re struggling — the ordinary ones specific to you (sleep, irritability, flatness, withdrawing, an extra drink)?
Your regulators: what genuinely settles you when things get hard? List the real ones, not the ones that should work.
Who is your one honest person — here or back home — you can tell the truth to? If you don’t have one here yet, how might you build that?
What professional or community support is available where you’re going — and what’s confidential? (Worth finding out before you need it.) one small thing

You’re not just relocating your life. You’re relocating yourself — and you’ve already begun.
