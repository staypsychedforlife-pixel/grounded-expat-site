The Repatriation Workbook

For coming home a different person than the one who left.
Created by Stephanie Johnson, LICSW

Everyone prepares you for going. Almost no one prepares you for coming back. You expected relief, the ease of the familiar, the simple comfort of home; instead you may feel restless, foreign, and quietly grieving in the one place that was supposed to feel effortless. This is reverse culture shock, and it is real, common, and often harder than the move abroad ever was.

This workbook helps you name the strange grief of coming home, make sense of feeling like a stranger in a familiar place, and slowly build a life here as the person you have become. This workbook is an educational, self-reflection resource, not therapy, diagnosis, treatment, or a crisis service. Reverse culture shock can look a lot like depression; if your low mood, numbness, or disorientation is persistent, severe, or comes with hopelessness, please reach out to a licensed professional or your local services. Coming home is a real transition, and you deserve real support through it.
Part One

The Re-Entry

The homecoming no one prepares you for

The Homecoming That Wasn’t

You pictured coming home as an exhale. Instead it can feel like landing in a place that no longer quite fits, missing a life you chose to leave, unsure why you are not simply happy to be back. That disorientation has a name, reverse culture shock, and naming it is the first relief.

A note for you

You are not ungrateful, and you have not made a mistake. Feeling unmoored at home is not a sign that you failed at coming back; it is a sign that you changed while you were away.

SFrom my work

In my work I see reverse culture shock catch people completely off guard, again and again. We brace ourselves for the foreign; we expect home to be easy. So when the familiar feels strangely hollow, there is no story ready to explain it, and people quietly conclude that something is wrong with them. There is not. The gap between the homecoming you imagined and the one you got is exactly where the grief lives, and grief that has a name is far easier to carry than grief that does not.

— Stephanie, LICSW

What did you expect coming home to feel like, and what has it actually felt like?
Where and when do you feel the disorientation most? Describe your own version of it.
What have you been telling yourself about why you are not simply glad to be back?
Say the honest sentence on paper: ‘Coming home has been harder than I expected, and that makes sense.’ What comes up when you do? one small thing

A Stranger in a Familiar Place

Sometimes home changed while you were gone. Sometimes it is almost exactly the same, and you are the one who is different. Either way, streets you have known your whole life can feel oddly foreign, and that mismatch is one of the loneliest parts of coming back.

A note for you

Feeling like a foreigner at home does not mean you do not belong. It means you are carrying more than one world inside you now, and it takes time for the familiar to feel familiar again.

Where do you feel most like a stranger right now, and where, if anywhere, do you still feel at home?
When you feel foreign at home, which is it more: that home changed, or that you changed?
Then part of the grief is that the home you carried in your memory is not the home you returned to.
What has changed here that you did not expect, and how does each change land in you?
What did you hold onto about home while you were away that turned out to be gone, or different than you remembered?
Then home is much the same, and the distance you feel is the distance you traveled on the inside.
How are you different now in ways the people and places here do not reflect back to you?
Which parts of who you became abroad feel invisible, or out of place, here?

The Change No One Sees

One of the hardest parts of coming home is that no one throws you a welcome-to-your-grief party. People expect the person who left; they do not see the one who came back. You get asked ‘how was your trip’ about years that quietly rearranged you.

A note for you

You are allowed to grieve a change that no one else can see. Your growth being invisible to others does not make it less real, and it does not mean you have to shrink back into who you used to be.

Who in your life expects the old you, and what is it like to meet that expectation?
What has changed in you that the people here have not noticed, or cannot quite understand?
Where have you found yourself performing the old version of you to keep others comfortable?
What is one thing you wish someone here truly understood about who you have become? one small thing
Part Two

Becoming Whole Here

Building a life as the person you’ve become

Homesick for There

There is a particular ache in being homesick for the place you left, especially the one you chose to leave. You may miss the streets, the friends, the food, the version of yourself who lived there. Longing for abroad does not cancel out being glad you came back; both can be true at once.

A note for you

Missing the life you left is not disloyalty to the life you are building. Two places can hold your heart at the same time, and grief is simply love with nowhere to go for the moment.

SFrom my work

People often expect their homesickness to point backward at a place, and they are surprised to find it pointing at a self. What we miss after coming home is rarely just a city; it is who we got to be there, the aliveness, the version of us that particular life made possible. In my experience this ache does not soften when we force ourselves to stop missing it. It softens when we let ourselves honor what that chapter gave us, and begin, slowly, to build some of that aliveness into the life we are in now.

— Stephanie, LICSW

What do you miss most about the life and the place you left? Be specific, and let it be tender.
What did that place, and the person you were there, give you that you do not want to lose?
How do you talk to yourself about missing it? Is there room to grieve without guilt?
What is one small way you could honor that chapter this week, rather than push it away? one small thing

The Friends Who Stayed

Coming home means walking back into relationships that kept going without you. Some friends slot right back in; others feel oddly distant, the shared context gone, the small talk somehow too small after everything you have lived. Reconnecting is its own quiet grief, and its own quiet hope.

A note for you

You are not cold or ungrateful for feeling out of step with people you love. Time and distance change relationships; they do not erase them. Some will find their rhythm again, and some will become something gentler and more occasional. Both are okay.

Who have you reconnected with since coming back, and how has it actually felt, easy, awkward, bittersweet?
When reconnecting feels hard, is it more that some friendships no longer fit, or that you feel out of step even in the good ones?
Then part of coming home is grieving relationships that have quietly changed shape.
Which friendships feel different now, and what changed between you?
What would it mean to let some become lighter, once-in-a-while connections, without guilt? one small thing
Then the friendships may be intact, and the distance is inside you, in everything you have lived that they cannot quite see.
What have you lived that feels almost impossible to explain to people who weren’t there?
Where could you find even one person who does get it, so you are not carrying it alone? one small thing

What You’re Keeping

You did not come home empty. Living abroad changes how you see, what you value, and who you are willing to be. Repatriation is not about erasing all of that to fit back in; it is about deciding, on purpose, what you carry forward into the life you are building here.

A note for you

You do not have to choose between who you were and who you became. The most grounded version of you gets to keep both, and to let your years away actually change your life here.

What did living abroad teach you about yourself that you want to keep?
What values, habits, or perspectives shifted while you were away, and which of them do you want to protect now?
Where do you feel pressure to abandon who you became just to fit back in, and from whom?
Name one thing from your life abroad you will deliberately carry into your life here. exercise

A Life Here, On New Terms

Coming home is not the end of the story; it is the start of a chapter you get to author. This last page turns everything you have named into a life here that fits the person you are now, not the one you were when you left.

A note for you

You do not have to slot back into your old life. You get to build a new one, here, on terms that honor everything you have lived. Home can be something you make, not only somewhere you return to.

What do you want home to mean for you now, as the person you have become?
As you think about home now, where are you?
Then this is about making here a real, chosen home, not a holding pattern.
What would make this place feel like a home you are choosing, rather than one you defaulted back into?
What is one thing you could build or seek out here that would help you belong again? one small thing
Then you are between chapters, and that uncertainty is allowed. You can be present here without deciding forever.
What would you need to feel settled enough to be present here, even if you are not staying for good?
What is the question about staying or going that you are sitting with, and who could you talk it through with? one small thing
Your coming-home plan, in three lines: the grief I will let myself feel is… / the thing from abroad I will keep is… / the one step toward belonging here I will take is… exercise
Who is one person you will let see the real, changed you, so you are not doing this alone?

You did not come home to become who you were. You came home to bring everything you have become, and to build a life worthy of it.
