Leaving Well

A guided journal for the goodbye.
Created by Stephanie Johnson, LICSW

You are in one of the strangest stretches of this whole thing. You have decided to go, and now you are living in the space between. Already halfway gone in your mind, still fully here in your body, saying goodbye to a life while you are somehow still inside it.

Nobody really prepares you for this part, so let's move through it together, gently, one page at a time. There are no right answers here. There is just the truth of what leaving actually feels like, and the permission to feel all of it. Your writing saves automatically on this device and stays private to you. This journal is an educational, self-reflection resource, not therapy, diagnosis, treatment, or a crisis service. If the grief of leaving stirs up something that feels too big to face alone, that is exactly what a good therapist is for; please reach out to a licensed professional or your local services. Feeling the whole of a goodbye is brave, and you do not have to carry the heaviest parts by yourself.
Phase One

It's Real Now

The decision settling into your body

When It Became Real

There is a moment when it stops being a beautiful maybe and becomes a thing that is actually happening. The ticket, the notice, the date on the calendar. And your body knows before your mind catches up. Suddenly it is real, and real feels different than the dream did.

A note for you

However you are feeling right now, thrilled or terrified or numb or all three at once, it is the right way to feel. There is no correct emotion for the edge of a leap. There is only yours.

SFrom my experience

The reality hit when I was sitting in an empty house with an extra large suitcase packed with everything left that I owned. Knowing that in one day I was never going to see that house again, and that the only belongings I had were in that suitcase. It was surreal, and honestly, sad.

— Stephanie, LICSW

When did it stop being a maybe and start being real for you? What did your body do in that moment, the flutter, the drop, the tears, the thrill?
Let yourself write what you are most excited about, with no hedging and no 'but.' What is the life on the other side of this that made you say yes?
Now the other side. What are you most afraid of? Write it plainly. Fear that gets named on a page is almost always smaller than fear that stays a shapeless weight in your chest.
Do you have moments of wanting to take it all back?
That does not mean you are making a mistake. The urge to undo it is not your wisdom, it is your nervous system reaching for the familiar because the familiar feels safe. You can want to run back to what you know and still be walking toward exactly the right thing.
What is the fear underneath the wanting-to-take-it-back?
What do you know, on a steadier day, that the fear conveniently forgets? one true thing
Steady certainty is its own kind of gift, and worth noticing, so you can reach for it on a harder day.
What is it that you know, deep down, that keeps the doubt quiet? Name it here.
Phase Two

The Last Times

Grieving the ordinary

The Ordinary You'll Miss

This is the part that surprises people. It is not the big landmarks you grieve. It is the ordinary. The walk you never thought twice about. The way the light comes into your kitchen. The barista who knows your order. The small, unremarkable textures of a life become almost unbearably precious once you know they are ending.

A note for you

It is not strange to grieve a lamp, a walk, a corner shop. The ordinary is where a life actually lives. Of course it is what you will miss.

SFrom my experience

I missed the smell of the Pacific Northwest ocean when the tide was out, Costco trips on Sundays for the samples, and seeing the mountains sink into the horizon as I drove down the highway. It was the missing of ordinary things I realized I had taken for granted, and also realizing how much I love home, and all of the beautiful memories I hold dear to my heart. Now when I do return home, I savor every second of it.

— Stephanie, LICSW

What ordinary, unremarkable thing about this place will you miss the most? Not the famous view. The small one.
Is there a 'last time' you want to do on purpose, with your whole attention, before you go? A last walk, a last meal somewhere, a last ordinary morning done slowly? one to plan
What made this place home? Try to name the actual ingredients, so you know what you are looking to build again on the other side.
Phase Three

The People

The goodbyes, and the guilt of the ones who stay

Who You're Leaving

Here is the hardest one. Leaving places is tender. Leaving people is grief. And it comes tangled up with guilt, the particular ache of being the one who gets to go while others stay, of choosing a life that puts distance between you and the people you love.

A note for you

Leaving people you love is not a betrayal of them. It is the cost of a life, and you are allowed to feel its full weight without letting it decide for you.

SFrom my work

As a therapist, here is what I want you to know about the guilt of going. Guilt is meant to signal that we have done something wrong. But wanting a fuller life is not wrong, and love is not a fixed amount that runs out with distance. The people who truly love you want you to have a life that is yours, even when your leaving costs them something. You can honor their loss and still get on the plane.

— Stephanie, LICSW

Who is the hardest to leave, and why? Let yourself be specific and let yourself feel it.
Notice the guilt, if it is there. What is it telling you? Now ask it, honestly: is that true? Is wanting your own life actually a betrayal of the people you love, or does it just feel that way right now?
Is there something you want to say to someone before you go that you have not said yet? Write it here first. Sometimes the page is where we find the words.
Is there someone who is not taking your leaving well?
Their sadness is real, and it is also not a verdict on your choice. Someone can be genuinely hurt that you are going and you can still be right to go.
What do you need to remember when their reaction gets heavy?
How can you hold their feelings with tenderness without letting them make this decision for you?
Being surrounded by people who are glad for you is a real gift, and worth letting in fully.
How can you let their support land, instead of bracing for a goodbye that is gentler than you feared?
Phase Four

The In-Between

The limbo, and the pre-flight wobble

Half Here, Half Gone

The final weeks have a strange, unreal quality. You are packing up a life. You are half-present at dinners because part of you is already gone. Your sense of who you are gets a little wobbly, because so much of identity is built from place and routine and people, and all three are dissolving at once. And somewhere in here, usually, comes the wobble: the 'what am I doing' panic that arrives right before something brave.

A note for you

The wobble is not a warning. It is what standing at the edge of something brave feels like. Fear shows up around what matters, around what would change us. If this were small, you would not feel it at all.

Where do you feel most caught between here and there right now? In what moments are you most 'already gone'?
It is tempting to check out early, to leave in your heart before you leave in your body. What would it cost you to stay present, and actually here, until the day you go? What might you miss if you leave early?
If the pre-flight wobble has arrived, write down exactly what your doubt is saying. Then read it back and ask: is this the truth, or is this just fear doing what fear does right before a leap? exercise
Phase Five

Carrying Them With You

Holding grief and excitement in the same hand

What You Take With You

Here is the thing I most want you to know before you go. You do not have to choose between the grief and the excitement. This is not a test where feeling sad means you are doing it wrong. And you do not actually leave the people and places behind. You carry them. They become part of who you are, wherever you land.

A note for you

You do not have to choose between the grief and the excitement. The fullest version of leaving holds both, the ache and the anticipation, in the very same hand.

How do you want to carry this place, and these people, with you? A ritual, an object, a habit, a way of staying in touch that you will actually keep? one to keep
Write a few lines to your future self, for a hard night abroad when the missing gets loud. Remind that version of you why you went, and what you already knew was worth it.
After all of this, name it: what are you ready for? Not what you are afraid of, not what you will miss. What is waiting for you that you are finally ready to go meet?

You are not abandoning your life. You are widening it. Leaving well does not mean leaving without grief. It means feeling the whole of it, saying your goodbyes with your whole heart, and walking toward the next thing anyway.
