The Partner’s Journal

For the partner in the move — building a life that’s also, unmistakably, your own.
Created by Stephanie Johnson, LICSW

This journal is entirely yours. Maybe the move leaned more toward someone else’s dream, or maybe you chose it together and it has cost you more than you expected — either way, your grief, your invisible work, and your becoming are your own, and they deserve a place to be named. It walks with you across the whole arc: the landing, the hard middle, the slow becoming, and carrying it home.

There’s no right way to use it. Write a lot or a little. Skip around. Come back to an entry months later and answer it again — you’ll be surprised what changes.

Your writing saves automatically on this device and stays completely private to you. Use Print / Save as PDF to keep a copy, or Download my writing to export everything you’ve written as a text file.

This journal is an educational, self-reflection resource — not therapy, diagnosis, treatment, or a crisis service. If you’re struggling in a way that feels beyond what journaling can hold, please reach out to someone you trust, a licensed professional, or your local emergency services. You deserve real support.
Phase One

Landing

Grief & the first weeks

Grief Without a Funeral

You left a whole life — a home, maybe a career, friendships, a version of yourself — and the world keeps treating it like an exciting adventure. There was no funeral for what you lost. But the grief is real, and it deserves a place to be named.

A note for you

What you’re feeling isn’t ingratitude, and it isn’t an overreaction. You’ve lost real things. Grief is simply the proof that they mattered.

SFrom my experience

Everyone celebrated the move.

They asked about the new country, the new job, and the adventure ahead. Almost no one asked what we had left behind.

There was no ceremony for the friendships, routines, or version of life that quietly ended when we boarded the plane.

For months, I thought feeling sad meant I wasn’t grateful. Eventually I realized I wasn’t ungrateful. I was grieving.

Some losses don’t come with funerals, but they deserve to be acknowledged just the same.

— Stephanie

What did you leave behind that you’re grieving most? Not what you think you should miss — what actually aches.
Who back home are you missing right now? What did they give you that you haven’t found here yet?
Has anyone acknowledged what this move cost you — or has it all been framed as exciting? How has that felt?
Where does the grief live in your body? Name it. Grief that goes unnamed tends to come out sideways — as fatigue, irritability, numbness. body · grounding
What’s one small way you could honor what you left — a ritual, a photo, a call, a meal from home — this week? one small thing

Your First Weeks

These first weeks are disorienting partly because your days can suddenly fill up with everyone else’s transition. This entry is about reclaiming a little ground that’s yours — your inventory, your goals, your support.

A note for you

Your needs didn’t get smaller because more of this move was about someone else. You’re allowed to take up space in your own life.

What are you most anxious about in the weeks ahead — the thing you haven’t said out loud yet?
What do you most need from your partner right now — that you’re not sure they know?
Picture your ideal day in this city six months from now — yours, not your partner’s, not the family’s. What are you doing? Who are you with? What does it feel like?
Name one professional, creative, or personal goal — just one — that’s yours for this chapter abroad.
What’s one concrete step you’ll take this week to begin meeting people here? Not a plan — an action. one small thing
Phase Two

The Hard Middle

Invisible work & identity

The Invisible Work

You’re the one who made the move work — the housing, the schools, the bureaucracy in a language you’re still learning, the emotional weather of the whole family. It’s enormous. And most of it is invisible, even to the people you’re doing it for.

A note for you

The work you’re doing is real work, even though no one’s paying you for it and no one can see it. Being the reason everyone else landed safely is not nothing — it’s everything.

Make the invisible visible: list what you’ve actually managed since the move — the logistics, the languages, the emotional labor. Don’t minimize. Write it all down.
Which part of this unseen work weighs on you most? What would it mean to have it acknowledged?
Where do you feel taken for granted — and could you say that out loud, to your partner or your family?
When you read your list back, what happens in your body? Notice it — that response is information about how much you’ve been carrying. body · grounding
What’s one thing you could hand off, share, or let be imperfect this week — to give yourself back a little capacity? one small thing

Identity on Hold

If you left a career or a role that told you who you were, there’s a specific grief in that — and a specific question: who am I here, without it? This entry sits with that honestly, and begins to build forward.

A note for you

You are not ‘just’ anyone’s partner. The role you paused is still in you — and the person underneath the role was always more than the title.

What does it feel like to not be fully known by anyone in your daily environment here? Try to name it precisely.
When you left, did you leave behind a career or role that told you who you were — or was your sense of self built more around other things?
Then there’s a specific grief here — losing the role that reflected you back. Let’s honor it, and find what travels.
What did your professional identity mean to you — beyond the paycheck or the title? What did it give you that you haven’t been able to replace here?
What parts of the professional person you were are still available to you, even without the role?
What’s one thing you could do this week that would be purely for you — not for your partner, not for your children, not for the move? one small thing
Then the question is quieter, but just as real: who were you underneath the roles — and who are you here?
If it wasn’t a job, what gave you your sense of self back home — and how much of that is available to you here?
What’s one thing you could do this week that would be purely for you — that reconnects you to that? one small thing
Phase Three

Becoming

Ambition & identity abroad

Reclaiming Ambition

At some point, surviving the move turns into a quieter, riskier question: what do I actually want now? Ambition can feel complicated to admit when your life is built around someone else’s. This is the place to admit it anyway.

A note for you

Wanting something for yourself isn’t a betrayal of your family or this move. Your ambition didn’t disappear — it’s been waiting for permission.

What are you afraid to want? What have you been holding back from admitting, even to yourself, because it feels complicated?
Do you already have a sense of what you want next — or is that still foggy?
Good — then this is about permission and a first step, not more figuring out.
What have you developed, built, navigated, or learned during this chapter that you’d put on a résumé if you knew how to frame it?
What’s the smallest real first step toward what you want? Not a plan — a single action you could take this week. one small thing
That’s okay — you don’t have to know yet. Let’s give the wanting some room to show itself.
If you had full permission and no practical constraints — what would you pursue during the rest of this chapter abroad? Don’t filter for realistic.
Where do you feel even a flicker of pull, curiosity, or envy lately? That’s often the first clue to what you want. one small thing

Your Identity Abroad

You’ve changed since you arrived — not just adapted, but changed. This entry is about noticing who you’re becoming, and choosing, on purpose, who you want to be in the second half.

A note for you

Who you’re becoming here is not a lesser version of who you were. You’ve done something hard, on unfamiliar ground, and you’re still standing. That’s worth claiming.

Describe yourself as you were six months ago — your life, your sense of self, what you were building, who you were in your own eyes.
What surprised you about yourself in the first months here — the things you didn’t expect?
What unexpected capacities have you discovered in yourself — things you didn’t know you had, or hadn’t needed to use before?
What qualities or ways of being do you want to actively develop in the second half of this chapter — for yourself, not for anyone else?
What’s one thing you’re going to do differently — starting now — that reflects who you’re choosing to become? one small thing
Phase Four

Carrying It Home

Re-entry & legacy

Coming Home

Going home sounds simple. It rarely is — especially for the partner who reshaped a whole life around this move and is now expected to slot back in. This entry helps you prepare for the real re-entry, not the postcard version.

A note for you

You’re not the same person who left, and home isn’t the same place. If re-entry feels destabilising, that’s not failure — it’s the cost of having grown.

Are you grieving anything about leaving this place — the community you built, the version of yourself you were becoming here?
Is returning home actually on your horizon soon — or is that still far off, unplanned, or not the plan?
Then let’s prepare for the real re-entry — the one that expects you to slot back in, when you’ve changed.
What are you actually anxious or ambivalent about regarding going home? Not the polished version — the real one.
What are you most afraid will be lost — about who you became here — when you step back into the old context?
Who do you want to be at home now, given who you’ve become here? Name one thing you’ll do differently. one small thing
Then ‘home’ is an open question — and for the partner who reshaped a life around this move, that’s worth sitting with honestly.
If you’re not going back soon, what do you want this next stretch to be for — now that the hardest part of the move is behind you?
What direction do you want to move in, professionally or creatively, from here? Even a rough sense counts. one small thing

Year One — Your Legacy

This is the capstone — a place to gather the whole year, honestly, into something you can keep. The cost and the gift, side by side. Take your time.

A note for you

You did something most people never attempt: you built a life on unfamiliar ground, largely for someone else, and found yourself in it anyway. Let yourself say what it cost — and what it gave.

What you gave up — and what it actually cost you, honestly. (Not ‘it was worth it’ yet. Just: what did it cost?)
What you built that you didn’t expect to — the things that surprised you.
What you know about yourself now that you didn’t know when you landed.
What you’d tell the person who said yes to this move — the one who was terrified and generous and didn’t know what was coming.
What’s one thing you want to carry forward from all of this, no matter where life takes you next? one small thing

Whatever this move asked of you — you are not invisible, and you are not alone.
