The Grounded Expat

The LGBTQ+ Couples Workbook

For two people holding a queer relationship together across a move.
Created by Stephanie Johnson, LICSW

A move abroad tests even strong relationships — and for a queer couple, it adds a layer most guides never mention. Alongside the usual strain of relocation, you’re navigating safety and visibility as a couple, laws that may not recognize your relationship, and the question of who you can be open with — all at once. This workbook helps you face that together, with honesty and care.

Each section has two parts: On your own — private reflection (you can each keep your own copy) — and Together — prompts to actually talk through as a couple. Your writing saves automatically on this device and stays private to you. Use Print / Save as PDF to keep a copy, or Download my writing to export it.

A note on privacy: your entries are stored only in this browser, on this device — never sent anywhere. If your situation makes privacy a matter of safety, you can clear them at any time, and it’s worth considering who else can access this device.

This workbook is an educational, self-reflection resource — not therapy, couples counseling, diagnosis, or a crisis service. If you are somewhere that being LGBTQ+ is criminalized or unsafe, your physical safety comes first. If your relationship is in serious distress, or if there is any harm, control, or fear in it, please reach out to an affirming couples therapist or your local support services. You both deserve real support, exactly as you are.
Part One

Where You Both Are

Seeing the move, and its risks, together

The Move You’re Both In

A move removes your usual supports, raises the daily stress, and asks you both to change at once — and for a queer couple, it also changes the ground beneath your relationship: how safe it is to be seen, how free you each feel here. This first section is about seeing the move you’re both actually in, from each side.

A note for you both

If this move has been hard on the two of you, that’s not a verdict on your relationship. Naming it together — out loud, without blame — is where it starts to get lighter.

On your own
How has this move actually landed for you — emotionally, and for the part of you that’s queer? Not the version you tell other people.
What has the move changed about how safe, free, or visible you feel as a couple here — compared to where you came from?
Together
Each of you, name one thing about this move that’s been harder than you expected — as a person and as a couple. Just listen to each other; no fixing, no defending. talk together

Whose Risk, Whose Visibility

Most queer relationships abroad carry an imbalance that’s easy to miss: often one partner is more out, more visibly queer, or simply carries more risk in this particular place — while the other moves through it differently. Add the usual asymmetry of any move (whose dream this was more, whose world this feels like), and there’s a lot to name. Doing it out loud helps enormously.

A note for you both

Acknowledging the imbalance isn’t assigning blame. It’s how the partner carrying more — of the risk, the caution, the invisible load — feels seen, and how you face it as a team instead of letting it become a wound.

On your own
Between the two of you, are you the one who’s more out or more visibly queer here — or the one who’s more cautious, or less out?
Then you may be carrying more of the exposure — and maybe a worry about what your visibility costs your partner. Let’s look at it.
What does being the more visible one cost or risk for you here — and what do you worry it costs your partner?
What do you most need from your partner around this — backup, discretion, shared decisions? Name it, even if you haven’t said it.
Then you may be carrying a quieter load — caution, or a gap between where you are and where your partner is with being out. Let’s name it honestly.
What does being the more cautious or less-out one cost you — and is there guilt, fear, or resentment in it you haven’t said?
What do you most need from your partner around this — patience, protection, not being pushed? Name it, even if it’s hard to say.
Together
Take turns: each name one thing the other carries — of the risk, the caution, or the invisible load — that you’re grateful for and haven’t said clearly. talk together
Part Two

Safe, Seen, and Steady

Visibility, strain & repair

Being ‘Us’ Out Loud

For a queer couple abroad, everyday things other couples don’t think about — holding hands, a photo, how you introduce each other — become decisions, sometimes safety decisions. Constantly monitoring your visibility is exhausting, and you may not even agree on where the line is. This section is for naming that, together.

A note for you both

Wanting to be openly a couple isn’t asking too much, and choosing discretion for safety isn’t hiding your love. You get to weigh visibility and safety together — and you’re allowed to grieve the freedom you don’t fully have here.

On your own
Where do you feel you can be openly a couple here, and where can’t you? How does that calculus sit with you?
What does the constant monitoring of your visibility cost you — the vigilance, the small denials, the grief? Where do you feel it most?
Together
Talk it through: where do you agree on how open to be, and where do you differ? Can you name one shared rule of thumb that respects both your safety and your need to be seen? talk together

Talking When You’re Both Depleted

Abroad you have less of everything that used to absorb stress — friends, family, routine, ease — and for a queer couple, add the load of vigilance, and the fact that home may be less of a refuge. More of it lands on the relationship, and you’re both running closer to empty. This is about communicating when neither of you has much left.

A note for you both

Snapping at each other when you’re both depleted isn’t proof the relationship is failing. It’s two tired nervous systems — carrying minority stress on top of a move — with nowhere else to put the strain. You can build a better release valve.

SFrom my work

In my work with queer couples, one of the most freeing realizations is that a lot of the friction isn’t really about each other — it’s the strain of navigating an unsafe or exhausting world with nowhere else to put it. When the world outside is hard on who you are, the relationship becomes the pressure valve. Naming that together — “this is the world’s weight, not ours” — often takes the sting out of the small fights, and lets you face the real source as a team.

— Stephanie, LICSW

On your own
What are your signs that you’re running on empty — and what do you actually need from your partner then (space, reassurance, practical help, just to be held)?
What’s a recurring tension between you since the move? What is it really about underneath — and how much of it is the world’s weight, not yours?
Together
Agree on a simple signal either of you can use to say “I’m running on empty — this isn’t the moment.” What will it be? talk together

Repair & Appreciation

Under stress, couples stop saying the good things and let small ruptures pile up. Reconnection isn’t grand — it’s appreciation said out loud and small repairs made on time. This section is for tending the bond, not just managing the strain.

A note for you both

You don’t need a perfect stretch to reconnect. You need a few honest words of appreciation and the willingness to repair small things before they calcify.

On your own
What do you genuinely appreciate about how your partner has handled this move — including the queer-specific parts — that you haven’t said lately?
Is there a small repair you owe, or one you’re hoping for? What would it take to make or receive it?
Together
Appreciation exchange: each tell the other three specific things you’re grateful for about them in this chapter. Then name one recurring argument you’d both like to retire. talk together
Part Three

Forward

Choosing each other, on purpose

Your Shared Agreement

You’ll do better with a few things said out loud and agreed, rather than assumed — and as a queer couple abroad, that includes some things straight couples never have to negotiate: how you’ll handle visibility and safety, legal or immigration realities, and being out (or not) to each other’s families. This last section turns it into a simple shared agreement.

A note for you both

A strong queer relationship abroad isn’t the one that never strains. It’s the one that keeps choosing each other — out loud, and on your own terms.

On your own
What would you need, concretely, to not just survive but actually thrive here — as a person, as a queer person, and as part of this couple?
Together
Write your shared agreement. Suggested lines to complete together: How we’ll make visibility and safety decisions together… / How we’ll handle legal or immigration realities… / How we’ll navigate being out (or not) to our families… / How we’ll share the invisible load… / One thing we’ll protect no matter what… talk together
Set one date in the next two weeks for a proper check-in — just the two of you, no logistics allowed. When? one small thing

Whatever this move asks of you, you don’t have to carry it alone — you have each other. Tend that, on purpose.
