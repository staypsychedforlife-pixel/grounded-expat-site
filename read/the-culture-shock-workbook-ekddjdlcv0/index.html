The Culture Shock Workbook

Understand it, settle it, and find your footing.
Created by Stephanie Johnson, LICSW

Culture shock is the most universal — and most misunderstood — part of moving abroad. It isn’t a sign you’ve made a mistake, and it isn’t weakness. It’s what happens when your nervous system has to consciously process a thousand things it used to handle on autopilot. This workbook helps you understand what’s actually happening, settle your system, and take small, real steps toward footing.

Part One

Understand It

What’s actually happening to you

What Culture Shock Actually Is

Culture shock isn’t rudeness toward your new home, and it isn’t proof you can’t do this. It’s the genuine exhaustion of a nervous system that has to think its way through what used to be automatic — the money, the manners, the language, the unspoken rules. Let’s start by naming where you are.

A note for you

If you’re not enjoying every minute of this ‘adventure,’ you are not failing at it. Culture shock is the normal, universal cost of moving between worlds — and naming it is the first relief.

SFrom my experience

A few months after we moved, I burst into tears because the grocery store didn’t have the yogurt I wanted.

It wasn’t about the yogurt. It was the first time I realized how exhausted I had become.

For months, my brain had been translating, adapting, problem-solving, and making thousands of decisions that used to happen automatically.

I thought I was becoming impatient. I was experiencing culture shock.

The moment I understood the difference, I stopped judging myself and started offering myself the same compassion I would have given anyone else adapting to a completely new life.

— Stephanie

What’s been hardest about being here that you didn’t expect — the thing that’s quietly worn you down?
Culture shock tends to move through stages — the early shine, the crash, the slow adjustment, and feeling at home. Where do you honestly feel you are right now?
What did you assume this would feel like — and how is the reality different from the picture you had?
Who is one person you could be honest with about how hard this actually is? Could you tell them this week? one small thing

The Daily Drain

Much of culture shock isn’t the big dramatic moments — it’s the constant, low-level translation. Every errand, conversation, and transaction costs a little more than it would at home. It adds up invisibly, until you’re depleted and can’t say why. Let’s make it visible.

A note for you

You’re not ‘too sensitive’ or ‘not cut out for this.’ You’re paying a hidden tax on your energy all day long. Seeing it is how you stop blaming yourself for being tired.

The translation audit: list the small, constant things that take more out of you here than they did at home — the grocery store, the bank, small talk, reading the room, the silence. Don’t filter; write them all. exercise
Which of these drains you the most — and why that one in particular?
On a scale of 1–10, how full is your tank at the end of an ordinary day here? What number feels true today? exercise
What’s one draining task you could simplify, batch, outsource, or skip entirely this week? one small thing

Your Body Knows First

Culture shock lives in the body before it reaches your thoughts. Your system reads ‘unfamiliar’ as ‘unsafe’ and stays subtly on guard — which surfaces as fatigue, poor sleep, irritability, flatness, or feeling wired. Learning your own signals turns them from frightening into useful.

A note for you

Your irritation and exhaustion aren’t character flaws. They’re a tired nervous system asking for care, not judgment.

When did you last feel dysregulated — irritable, tearful, flat, or wired? What was happening right before?
Right now, is your system more wired — anxious, on edge, can’t settle — or more flat: numb, low, checked out?
A wired system needs downshifting — signals of safety, not more input.
Where does ‘wired’ show up in your body — jaw, chest, shoulders, stomach? Rest a hand there. What is it asking for? body · grounding
What actually helps you come down — not what should, but what does? (Slow breath, a walk, water, quiet, a familiar voice.) List your real ones.
What’s one way you could build a little more rest or predictability into this week? one small thing
A flat system doesn’t need pushing — it needs gentle, low-effort reconnection.
What does ‘flat’ feel like from the inside right now — and when did it start?
What’s the smallest thing that has reliably given you even a flicker of feeling or energy here? Coffee counts.
What’s one tiny, low-effort act of care you could do today — not to fix the flatness, just to be kind to it? one small thing
Part Two

Settle It

Bringing your system back to baseline

Building Your Regulation Toolkit

You can’t think your way out of a dysregulated nervous system — you settle it first, then think. This is about assembling a small, reliable kit of things that bring you back to baseline, and enough predictability that your system can finally lower its guard.

A note for you

Routines aren’t rigid or boring — abroad, they’re medicine. Predictability is how your nervous system learns it’s safe here.

Your regulation menu: list what genuinely steadies you — fast ones (a walk, a song, a few slow breaths) and slower ones (a bath, a call home, time outdoors). Keep this where you’ll find it on a hard day. exercise
What one small daily routine could you protect no matter what — an anchor that stays the same every day?
Where in your week could you build in more rest or quiet — even twenty minutes that are reliably yours?
What’s one regulating thing from your menu you’ll actually do today? one small thing

Homesickness & the Comparison Trap

Homesickness isn’t weakness — it’s love, displaced. And the phone makes it harder: scrolling everyone’s ordinary life back home can convince you that you’re missing the ‘real’ thing while you struggle. Both stories — ‘I’m missing out’ and ‘I should just be grateful’ — are distortions.

A note for you

You’re not missing your old life; you’re living a different one. Both are real. Neither cancels the other.

What do you miss most about home right now? Let yourself actually feel it, without rushing to ‘but I’m lucky.’
Which story runs louder for you — ‘I’m missing out on the real thing back home,’ or ‘I should just be grateful, so I shouldn’t even feel this’?
Then the phone is probably making it worse — comparison feeds on everyone’s highlight reel. Let’s put some guardrails on it.
When does the ‘missing out’ feeling hit hardest — a time of day, the phone, a particular situation?
What’s one boundary with your phone or social media that would protect you this week? one small thing
Then the trap is guilt — telling yourself you have no right to struggle. Let’s gently take that apart.
Can you let two things be true at once: you’re genuinely lucky to be here, AND this is genuinely hard? Write both, side by side.
Who are the two or three people back home you most want to stay genuinely connected to? What would staying close ‘on purpose’ look like? one small thing

Finding Your Footing

Settling isn’t a single decision — it’s a series of small experiments. Belonging grows in repeated, ordinary places (the same café, the same class, the same faces), and confidence grows from tiny wins. This last section turns insight into a gentle plan.

A note for you

You don’t have to love it here yet, or feel at home overnight. You just have to take one small step toward footing — and then another.

Where do you already feel even a flicker of ease or belonging here? What’s creating it?
Name one repeated context — a class, a group, a regular spot — you could return to enough times to become a familiar face.
What’s one small experiment you could run this week to make this place feel five percent more like yours? one small thing
