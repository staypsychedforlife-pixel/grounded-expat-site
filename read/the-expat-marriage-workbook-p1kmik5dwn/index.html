The Grounded Expat

The Expat Marriage Workbook

For two people holding a relationship together across a move.
Created by Stephanie Johnson, LICSW

A move abroad tests even strong relationships — not because something’s wrong with you as a couple, but because it strips away your usual supports, raises the daily stress, and asks you both to change at once. This workbook helps you face that together, with honesty and care.

Each section has two parts: On your own — private reflection (you can each keep your own copy), and Together — prompts to actually talk through as a couple. This workbook is an educational, self-reflection resource — not therapy, couples counseling, diagnosis, or a crisis service. If your relationship is in serious distress, or if there is any harm, control, or fear in it, please reach out to a licensed couples therapist or your local support services. You both deserve real support.
Part One

Where You Both Are

Seeing the move from both sides

The Move You’re Both In

A move removes your usual supports, raises the daily stress, and asks you both to change at the same time. Strain, in that context, isn’t a sign you chose wrong — it’s a sign you’re carrying something heavy. This first section is about seeing the move you’re both actually in, from each side.

A note for you both

If this move has been hard on the two of you, that’s not a verdict on your relationship. Naming it together — out loud, without blame — is where it starts to get lighter.

On your own
How has this move actually landed for you — emotionally, not the version you tell other people?
Where do you feel the two of you have drifted, or rubbed against each other, since the move?
Together
Each of you, name one thing about this move that’s been harder than you expected. Just listen to each other — no fixing, no defending. talk together

The Invisible Imbalance

Many moves are asymmetric: one person’s opportunity, or dream, or restlessness drove it a little more, and the other reshaped their life around it. Even when you chose it together, that imbalance — in career, in daily load, in whose world this feels like — is one of the biggest quiet strains on an expat relationship. Naming it out loud helps enormously.

A note for you both

Acknowledging the imbalance isn’t assigning blame. It’s how the partner who gave up more feels seen — and how you face it as a team instead of letting it become a wound.

On your own
Which side of this move do you feel more on — the partner whose dream or opportunity drove it a little more, or the one who reshaped their life around it? (Or does it shift depending on the day?)
Then you may be carrying a quieter guilt — that your partner gave up more. Let’s look at it honestly.
What has your partner given up or taken on because of this move that you may not fully let yourself see?
Where do you feel guilt, or a pressure to make this ‘worth it’ for both of you? Be honest; this page is yours.
Then you may be carrying things your partner can’t fully see. Let’s name them.
What have you given up or taken on because of this move that your partner may not fully see?
Where do you feel guilt, resentment, or taken for granted — even a little? Be honest; this page is yours.
Together
Take turns: each name one thing the other has carried or sacrificed that you’re grateful for and haven’t said clearly. talk together
Part Two

How You Talk & Repair

Protecting the bond under strain

Talking When You’re Both Depleted

Abroad you have less of everything that used to absorb stress — friends, family, routine, ease — so more of it lands in the relationship, and you’re both running closer to empty. Conflicts that would’ve blown over at home can stick. This is about communicating when neither of you has much left.

A note for you both

Snapping at each other when you’re both depleted isn’t proof the relationship is failing. It’s two tired nervous systems with nowhere else to put the strain. You can build a better release valve.

On your own
What are your signs that you’re running on empty — and what do you actually need from your partner then (space, reassurance, practical help)?
What’s a recurring tension between you since the move? What is it really about, underneath the surface argument?
Together
Agree on a simple signal either of you can use to say “I’m running on empty — this isn’t the moment.” What will it be? talk together

Too Much Weight on One Bridge

When you’ve left your wider support behind, your partner can quietly become your only friend, confidant, therapist, and social life all at once. That’s too much weight for any one relationship to carry — and it’s a common, fixable cause of strain abroad.

A note for you both

Needing more than your partner alone can give isn’t a failure of the relationship. It’s a sign you both need a wider world — and building it protects the two of you.

On your own
What are you currently relying on your partner for that you used to get from other people or places?
Where do you most need connection or support beyond your relationship right now?
Together
Where could you each widen your support base — friendships, community, help — so the relationship isn’t carrying all of it? Name one step each. talk together

Repair & Appreciation

Under stress, couples stop saying the good things and let small ruptures pile up. Reconnection isn’t grand — it’s appreciation said out loud and small repairs made on time. This section is for tending the bond, not just managing the strain.

A note for you both

You don’t need a perfect stretch to reconnect. You need a few honest words of appreciation and the willingness to repair small things before they calcify.

On your own
What do you genuinely appreciate about how your partner has handled this move — that you haven’t said lately?
Is there a small repair you owe, or one you’re hoping for? What would it take to make or receive it?
Together
Appreciation exchange: each tell the other three specific things you’re grateful for about them in this chapter. Then name one recurring argument you’d both like to retire. talk together
Part Three

Forward

Choosing each other, on purpose

Your Shared Agreement

You’ll do better with a few things said out loud and agreed, rather than assumed. This last section turns everything into a simple shared agreement — how you’ll check in, share the load, protect the relationship, and make sure both of your lives matter here, not just the one the move was for.

A note for you both

A strong expat relationship isn’t the one that never strains. It’s the one that keeps choosing each other, on purpose, out loud.

On your own
What would you need, concretely, to not just survive but actually thrive here — as a person, and as part of this couple?
Together
Write your shared agreement. Suggested lines to complete together: How we’ll check in with each other (and how often)… / How we’ll share the invisible load… / How we’ll make sure both of our lives and careers matter… / One thing we’ll protect no matter what… talk together
Set one date in the next two weeks for a proper check-in — just the two of you, no logistics allowed. When? one small thing

Whatever this move asks of you, you don’t have to carry it alone — you have each other. Tend that, on purpose.
