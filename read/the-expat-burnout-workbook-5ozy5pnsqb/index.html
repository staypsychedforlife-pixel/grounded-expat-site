The Expat Burnout Workbook

For when you’re exhausted abroad and can’t name why.
Created by Stephanie Johnson, LICSW

There’s a particular exhaustion that comes from living abroad — not the dramatic kind, but a deep, cumulative depletion from adapting all day, every day, to a world that doesn’t run on autopilot for you. Add the overfunctioning many of us do to prove we can handle it, and you get burnout that hides in plain sight: you’re tired, flat, and ‘shouldn’t be,’ because on paper your life is enviable.

Part One

Name It

Making the invisible depletion visible

The Exhaustion No One Sees

Expat burnout rarely announces itself. It builds from the invisible tax of constant adaptation — the language, the admin, the unspoken rules, the being ‘on’ — until you’re running on empty and can’t point to a single big reason why. Naming it is the first relief.

A note for you

You’re not weak, ungrateful, or ‘not cut out for this.’ You’re depleted from doing something genuinely effortful every single day — and naming it is not complaining, it’s care.

SFrom my experience

I remember sitting in a café in Thailand, staring at a cup of coffee I had been excited to try all week. I had finally made it to this beautiful little spot everyone recommended. The weather was perfect. I had the freedom I had dreamed about.

And yet all I wanted to do was go home.

The problem was… I didn’t know where ‘home’ was anymore.

Nothing had gone wrong. I wasn’t in crisis. I was just profoundly tired in a way sleep couldn’t fix. Every simple task still required mental effort. Every conversation happened in a language or culture I had to consciously think through. Every day demanded that I adapt.

But I felt guilty admitting it. I’d chosen this life. I was living what many people considered a dream. What right did I have to feel exhausted?

Looking back, I realize I wasn’t weak. I was carrying the invisible cognitive and emotional load of building a life from scratch — the kind of exhaustion relocation creates, the kind no one sees because, from the outside, everything looks exciting.

— Stephanie

How does your burnout actually show up — the flatness, the short fuse, the dread, the exhaustion that sleep doesn’t fix? Describe your version.
When did you first notice you weren’t okay? What have you been telling yourself about it since?
What makes it hard to admit you’re burnt out — the guilt of a ‘privileged’ life, the fear of seeming like you can’t cope?
Say the true sentence on paper: ‘I am exhausted, and it makes sense.’ What comes up when you do? one small thing

Where Your Energy Actually Goes

You can’t refill a tank you can’t see draining. Burnout eases when we make the invisible costs visible — the tasks, roles, and worries quietly eating your energy — and notice how little goes back in. This is an honest audit, not a judgment.

A note for you

Seeing where your energy goes isn’t self-indulgent — it’s the only way to spend it on purpose instead of watching it disappear.

The energy audit: list everything that drains you right now — the daily adaptation, work, admin, relationships, worries. Don’t filter. exercise
Now the other column: what genuinely refills you? Be honest about how often you actually get any of it.
What is the gap between the two columns telling you?
Which drain is biggest — and is any of it something you’ve taken on that isn’t truly yours to carry?

The Overfunctioner

Many expats burn out not just from adapting, but from overfunctioning — doing more, proving more, holding it all together so no one worries, so the move looks ‘worth it.’ It’s often an old pattern the move amplified. Seeing it is how you start to put some of it down.

A note for you

You don’t have to earn your place here by being tireless. You’re allowed to do less, ask for help, and let some things be ‘good enough’ without the sky falling.

Where do you overfunction — taking on too much, holding it together for everyone, refusing help, proving you can cope?
Is this overfunctioning a new thing the move brought on, or an old pattern that just got louder?
Then it’s situational — the move raised the stakes. Let’s ease the pressure.
What is it about being abroad that makes you feel you have to hold it all together — proving the move was worth it, not worrying people back home, no one here to share the load?
What’s one thing you could let be ‘good enough,’ or hand off, this week? one small thing
Then the move didn’t create it — it amplified something older. Seeing that is how you start to put it down.
Where did you first learn that rest had to be earned, or that holding it all together was your job?
Who are you still trying to prove something to — and what are you afraid they’d think if you did less?
What’s one small permission you could give yourself this week that the old pattern wouldn’t allow? one small thing
Part Two

Refill It

Rebuilding a pace you can keep

Rest That Actually Restores

Not all rest restores — scrolling and numbing pass the time without refilling you. Real recovery is specific and, abroad, often deliberate: the things that genuinely bring you back to yourself. This is about knowing yours and protecting them.

A note for you

Rest isn’t a reward you get after you’ve earned it. It’s the thing that makes everything else possible — and you’re allowed to have it now, not later.

SFrom my experience

When I first moved abroad, I thought rest meant escaping.

I’d spend hours scrolling social media, binge-watch Netflix, or disappear into YouTube, hoping I’d wake up feeling more like myself.

I never did.

The turning point came when I stopped trying to numb the discomfort and started creating small moments that felt familiar. A morning walk on the same route. Finding one café where the barista recognized me. Reading a book instead of consuming endless information about my new country. Calling a friend without talking about logistics or visas.

None of those things were dramatic. But they reminded my nervous system that it was safe.

I’ve learned there’s a difference between distraction and restoration. One helps you forget how tired you are for a little while. The other slowly helps you become yourself again — and for people living abroad, that distinction can make all the difference.

— Stephanie

When was the last time you felt genuinely restored — not just distracted? What were you doing?
What passes as ‘rest’ for you but actually leaves you emptier — doomscrolling, numbing, ‘productive’ downtime?
List the things that truly refill YOU — sensory, social, solitary, creative, physical. Keep it where you’ll see it. exercise
What’s one genuinely restorative thing you’ll give yourself this week — and when? one small thing

Boundaries & Saying No Abroad

Abroad, boundaries get slippery — you say yes to every invitation so you’re not lonely, take on more at work to prove yourself, stay endlessly available across time zones. Burnout recovery needs a few honest no’s to protect the yes’s that matter.

A note for you

Every yes you give out of guilt or fear is a no to your own recovery. Protecting your energy isn’t rude — it’s how you stay someone who has anything left to give.

Where are you saying yes when you mean no — socially, at work, with family back home?
What makes the boundaries hardest right now — the fear of missing out, or the fear of disappointing people?
Then this is about scarcity — the sense that if you say no, you’ll be left out or left behind.
When you say yes out of not-wanting-to-miss-out, what does it actually cost you — energy, rest, time that was meant to be yours?
What’s one small ‘no,’ or one limit, you’ll practice this week to protect the yes’s that matter? one small thing
Then this is about other people’s feelings — and your sense that protecting your energy will let them down.
Whose disappointment are you most afraid of — and is that fear as true, or as catastrophic, as it feels?
Draft one kind, clear way to say no that protects your energy without over-explaining or apologizing. exercise

Your Sustainable Pace

Burnout doesn’t lift with one good weekend — it lifts when your daily life stops asking more than it gives back. This last page turns everything you’ve noticed into a pace you can actually keep, and a few early-warning signs so you catch it sooner next time.

A note for you

You don’t have to overhaul your whole life. You have to tilt it, gently and repeatedly, back toward sustainable — and trust that small, kept changes add up.

What has to change — even a little — for your life here to stop draining you faster than it fills you?
What are your earliest warning signs that you’re heading toward empty again? How will you catch it sooner?
Your sustainable-pace plan, in three lines — the drain I’ll reduce is… / the thing that refills me I’ll protect is… / the boundary I’ll hold is… exercise
Who is one person you’ll let in on how you’re really doing, so you’re not carrying it alone?

You were never meant to run on empty to deserve this life — and a steadier pace is something you can begin today.
