The Grounding Kit

Your first 90 days abroad, held.

Hi, and welcome. If you are opening this, you have either just landed somewhere new or you are about to, and either way, I am so glad you are here.

I made this kit because the first months abroad are so much more than logistics. Everyone hands you a checklist for the SIM card and the bank account. Almost no one prepares you for the homesickness that hits at 2am, the strange grief of a life you chose, or the dip around week three when you are certain you made a mistake. I am a therapist, and a nomad, and I have lived all of it. This is what I wish someone had handed me.

Nothing here is meant to be done all at once. Wander through it. Come back to the pieces you need on the days you need them. Let it be a companion, not another thing to keep up with.

Take good care of yourself over there. — Stephanie, LICSW

05The 90-day rhythm map

Not a schedule to keep up with. A map, so that when the ground shifts, you know roughly where you are and that you are not lost.

Weeks 1–2

Landing

Adrenaline and disorientation, thrilled and exhausted at once. Lower the bar all the way. The goal is not a great day, it is a small one. This is not the season to judge whether you did the right thing. Lean on: the Landing Checklist and the "Landing" section of your Relocation Journal. Keep the 2am Card close.

Weeks 3–8

The Hard Middle

The shine wears off. Culture shock, small frictions, loneliness that can get loud. Around six weeks many people hit a dip and think, "I made a mistake." That feeling is real, but it is usually the dip talking. No big decisions right now. Keep saying yes, keep showing up. Lean on: the Culture Shock Workbook, the audios, and Reflect With Me. This is the phase the kit was really built for.

Weeks 9–12

Becoming

Quieter. One day the walk home just feels like yours. A regular, a routine, maybe a person or two. Notice it, because belonging rarely arrives in a big moment. Lean on: the "Becoming" section of your journal, and a look back at your early entries to see how far you have come.

06The Landing Checklist

Not because logistics are the point, but because competence calms panic. When you know how to move, feed yourself, and get help, the whole place goes from overwhelming to manageable.

  • First 48 hours
  • Data in your pocket. A local SIM or eSIM, or a working roaming plan.
  • Cash and a card that works. Tell your home bank you have moved so nothing gets frozen.
  • Know your way home. Save your address in the local language and screenshot it.
  • Water and a first food run. You do not have to cook today. You just have to eat.
  • Emergency numbers saved. The local 911, plus the nearest hospital.
  • First week
  • One full grocery run. This one errand quietly makes the place feel like yours.
  • The pharmacy. Find the nearest one and restock anything you take regularly.
  • Transit sorted. The card, the app, the one line you will use most.
  • Wifi and a power setup, plus the right plug adapters.
  • A third place, chosen on purpose. One cafe, park, or gym you return to. This is how belonging begins.
  • First month
  • Registration and paperwork. Find the deadline and put it in your calendar.
  • A doctor or clinic you could call. Knowing where you would go is its own kind of calm.
  • A standing call home. Same day, same time, so the missing has a container.
  • One local thing you actually enjoy. Not a task. A reason to be glad you are here.

The 2am Card

For the nights the missing gets loud. If you are reading this at 2am, homesick and wide awake, nothing has gone wrong. This is expected when we are far from what we know and love. It is a heart doing exactly what hearts do.

Put both feet flat on the floor. Feel the ground actually holding you up. It is, even now, even here.
Breathe in for four, out for six. Three times. The longer exhale tells your nervous system it is safe to settle.
Name five things you can see. This pulls you out of the 3am story and back into the safe present.
"I can miss home and still belong to this life. Both are true. I do not have to fix this tonight."

You do not have to solve anything at 2am. Morning always comes, and things look different in the light. You are doing something genuinely hard, and you are doing it. Rest now.

— Stephanie, LICSW
