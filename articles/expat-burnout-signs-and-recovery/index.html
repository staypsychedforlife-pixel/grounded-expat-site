The inner side of moving abroad

Expat Burnout

By Stephanie Johnson, LICSW — licensed clinical social worker & expat

You moved abroad for a bigger life, and lately you're too exhausted to enjoy any of it. Not tired in a way a weekend fixes — a bone-deep depletion, a flatness, a sense of running on fumes. That isn't you being weak or ungrateful. It's expat burnout, and it's one of the most common and least-named parts of living abroad.

As a therapist who has also lived it, here's what expat burnout actually is, why life abroad quietly drains you, and what genuinely helps you refill.

What expat burnout actually is

Burnout isn't ordinary tiredness. It's what happens when your system spends too long in output mode without enough that restores it. Abroad, the drain is constant and mostly invisible: you're adapting every waking hour — decoding a language, a culture, a currency, an unfamiliar way that everything works — with none of the autopilot that used to carry you through an ordinary day. Even good days cost more than they would have back home.

Why living abroad is uniquely draining

At home, most of your life runs on autopilot — you know the roads, the norms, how to be understood without thinking about it. Abroad, all of that becomes effortful. Ordering coffee, reading a room, sorting out a bill: each one takes conscious energy, and underneath it runs a low hum of hypervigilance, your nervous system quietly braced for the next thing you won't understand. Culture shock isn't only an emotion; it's a genuine cognitive load, running in the background all day. No wonder you're depleted.

The overfunctioner's trap

Burnout hits hardest for the person holding everything together — and abroad, that's often whoever took on settling the family: the housing, the schools, the admin, the emotional weather of everyone else's adjustment. (If that's you, you may recognize yourself in the trailing spouse.) Overfunctioning can feel responsible, even noble. But run long enough with no one refilling you, and it empties you right out.

Burnout isn't a sign you can't handle life abroad. It's a sign you've been handling too much of it, alone, for too long.

The signs you're running on empty

  • Exhaustion that sleep doesn't fix.
  • A short fuse — irritability that isn't really like you.
  • Numbness or flatness where there used to be feeling.
  • Dreading things you used to genuinely enjoy.
  • Resentment leaking out sideways.
  • A grim "just push through" as your only available setting.
  • Getting sick more, or everything simply feeling like too much.

Why gritting through it makes it worse

The instinct is to push harder — you're already behind, people are depending on you, you chose this life. But burnout doesn't respond to grit; grit is often what caused it. Pushing through an empty tank doesn't refill it — it grinds the engine. The way out isn't more effort. It's less output, and more genuine restoration.

What actually helps

Recovering from burnout abroad isn't about doing more. It's about a few deliberate shifts:

  • Rest that restores, not just distracts. Scrolling numbs; it doesn't refill. Find the things that actually put energy back — quiet, nature, real sleep, a conversation that feeds you.
  • Drop the overfunctioning. Hand back what isn't yours to carry, and let some things be imperfect. Your family will survive a lower standard. You might not survive keeping it.
  • Choose connection over isolation. Burnout and loneliness feed each other. You can't grit your way out alone — you refill through people who understand.
  • Lower the bar, on purpose. "Good enough" is a survival skill abroad, not a failure.
  • Get support before you're in crisis. A community, a friend, a professional — you don't have to wait until you're completely flattened to reach for help.

Refilling is not quitting

Choosing to rest, to hand things back, to reach for support — none of that means you're failing at life abroad. It means you're finally treating yourself like a person with limits instead of a machine. The bigger life you moved for is still there, waiting for you. You just can't pour it from an empty cup. Refill first, and almost everything else gets easier from there.

Common questions

What is expat burnout?

Expat burnout is a deep depletion caused by the constant, often invisible effort of adapting to life in a new country — decoding an unfamiliar language, culture, and system with none of the autopilot of home, usually while holding a lot together. It shows up as exhaustion sleep doesn't fix, irritability, numbness, and dread.

What are the signs of burnout while living abroad?

Exhaustion that rest doesn't fix, a short fuse, emotional numbness, dreading things you used to enjoy, growing resentment, and a grim "just push through" as your only mode. Getting sick more often and feeling like everything is too much are common too.

How do I recover from expat burnout?

Prioritize rest that actually restores (not just distraction), hand back the overfunctioning, choose connection over isolation, lower your standards on purpose, and reach for support before you hit crisis. Grit is what caused it — less output and real restoration is the way out.

This article is educational and reflective — not therapy, diagnosis, or a crisis service. If burnout has tipped into depression, hopelessness, or thoughts of harming yourself, please reach out to a licensed professional or a crisis line right away. You deserve real support.
