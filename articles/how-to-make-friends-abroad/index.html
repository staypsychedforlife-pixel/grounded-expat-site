How to Make Friends Abroad When You're Starting Over From Zero

By Stephanie Johnson, LICSW, a licensed clinical social worker and expat

Nobody warns you that moving abroad means rebuilding your entire social world from scratch. The friendships that knew you without you having to explain yourself are far away, and here you are, a grown adult, trying to make brand new ones from zero, often in a second language.

If you are lonely right now, I want to say this first, before anything practical: you are not doing this wrong. Making friends abroad is genuinely one of the hardest, least talked about parts of the whole experience. The loneliness you feel is not a character flaw. It is the completely normal cost of doing something brave.

Why making friends abroad is so hard

Adult friendship is already hard for everyone, everywhere. As kids we made friends inside built-in structures, school, sports, the same faces every day. As adults, most of us keep the friends we made back then and rarely have to start over. Moving abroad strips all of that away at once.

On top of the ordinary difficulty, a few things stack up when you are new to a country:

  • No shared history. The people around you have known each other for years. You are walking into rooms where the connections are already formed.
  • Language and cultural rhythm. Even small talk takes more effort in another language, and the unspoken rules of how friendship works can be different from what you grew up with.
  • You are doing everything else too. You are also learning a new city, new admin, a new job or routine. There is only so much of you to go around.
  • The stories in your head. "Everyone here already has their people." "I am too old to start over." "If they wanted to hang out, they would have asked." Those thoughts feel like facts, and they keep you home.

If you are lonely, you have not failed

When the loneliness is loud, the mind offers the cruelest explanation on hand: something must be wrong with me. It is worth naming that this is the vulnerable early stretch of a very normal arc, not a verdict on who you are. Loneliness after a move is so common that I wrote a whole piece on it: coping with expat loneliness.

You are not behind at making friends. You are starting a whole social life over, as an adult, in a new place. That is not a small thing, and it does not happen in a week.

How to make friends abroad, gently

You do not build a community in one grand gesture. You build it in small, repeatable, slightly brave moments. Here is what actually helps.

  1. Find your third place. Not home, not work, but one café, gym, class, or park you return to often enough that the faces start to recognize yours. Belonging is built through repetition. Pick one place and keep showing up.
  2. Say yes for the first while. Early on, say yes to almost everything, even the plans that sound only slightly appealing. You are not committing to a best friend, you are widening the pool. A few of those lukewarm invitations become the people you lean on later.
  3. Give every friendship three tries. New connection rarely sparks on the first hang. So much of it lives in the second and third meeting, once the awkwardness settles. If someone seems kind, invite them again before you decide it is not clicking.
  4. Be the one who follows up. Waiting to be chosen keeps you lonely. Almost everyone abroad is quietly hoping someone else will make the first move. Be that person. A simple "I would love to do this again, are you free next week?" does more than you think.
  5. Look for your people, not everyone. You do not need a huge social life. You need a small handful of people you can be real with. Chase depth over numbers, and let the surface acquaintances be exactly what they are.
  6. Let yourself be a little more known. Connection deepens when you risk being seen. You do not have to overshare, but offering one true thing, that you are finding this hard, that you miss home some days, gives the other person permission to be real too.
  7. Lower the bar on the hard days. On the days when reaching out feels like too much, make the ask tiny. One message. One coffee. One walk. Showing up small still counts, and it is almost always the thing that turns the day.

Belonging is built slowly

If you take one thing from this, let it be that the quiet, friendless stretch is not permanent and it is not proof of anything about you. It is the ordinary middle of building a life somewhere new. The barista who starts to know your order, the acquaintance who becomes a friend on the third try, the first time someone here texts you just to check in, these accumulate quietly, and then one day you look up and you are not starting from zero anymore. Be patient and be brave in small ways. Your people are out there, and they are looking for someone like you too.

Common questions

How do you make friends in a new country as an adult?

Through repetition and small risks, not one big effort. Pick a regular spot or a recurring activity, say yes to invitations early, follow up rather than waiting to be chosen, and give each budding friendship two or three tries before deciding. Depth grows slowly, so consistency matters more than charisma.

How long does it take to make friends abroad?

It varies, but for many people the first few months are the loneliest, and real friendships start to form somewhere in the three-to-six-month range as you keep showing up. If it is taking longer than you hoped, that is common and not a sign you are doing it wrong.

Is it normal to feel lonely after moving abroad?

Very. Loneliness is one of the most common experiences of the early months abroad, precisely because you have left your whole social world behind and have to rebuild it. It does not mean you made the wrong choice, and it does ease as you settle in.

This article is educational and reflective, not therapy, diagnosis, or a crisis service. If you are struggling beyond what a journal can hold, please reach out to someone you trust, a licensed professional, or local emergency services. You deserve real support.
