The inner side of moving abroad

The Trailing Spouse

By Stephanie Johnson, LICSW — licensed clinical social worker & expat

There's a phrase that shows up in relocation paperwork and almost nowhere else: trailing spouse. It's the person who moves abroad not for their own job, but for their partner's — the one who lets go of a career, a community, and a whole familiar life so someone else can take an opportunity. If that's you, you already know the term doesn't capture the half of it.

As a therapist who has also lived abroad, I want to name what the trailing spouse actually carries — because so much of it goes unseen, even by the person carrying it.

The identity you left at the airport

When you move for your own job, you land with an identity intact: a role, a title, colleagues, a reason to be there. When you move for someone else's, you often arrive as no one in particular — the spouse of, the plus-one on the visa. The work that used to tell you who you were is gone, and nothing has replaced it yet. That's not a small adjustment. For a lot of people it's a genuine grief, and it deserves to be treated like one.

The invisible labor no one sees

While your partner steps into a structured job, you're often the one holding everything else together — finding the housing, the schools, the doctors, the grocery store that carries the thing your kid will actually eat. You become the family's translator, project manager, and emotional shock absorber, usually in a language and system you're still learning yourself. It's enormous, skilled work. And because it doesn't come with a paycheck or a title, it's easy for everyone — including you — to act as though it isn't work at all.

This is the imbalance that quietly wears on a lot of expat relationships, too. If it's straining yours, that's worth naming out loud together — I wrote more about that in when moving abroad strains your marriage.

You didn't just move to a new country. You set down an entire version of yourself — and no one handed you a map for building the next one.

The particular loneliness

Your partner's world comes with people built in. Yours doesn't. They spend the day surrounded by colleagues while you navigate the new place mostly alone, and then they come home drained and you're the one who's been starving for adult conversation. It's a specific kind of isolation, and it can make you feel needy or ungrateful for wanting more — which just adds guilt on top of the loneliness.

You're allowed to grieve a move you agreed to

Here's the thing that trips people up: you said yes to this. You may even have been genuinely excited. So when it turns out to be hard, there's a voice that says you have no right to struggle — you chose it, so you should be grateful. But agreeing to something doesn't mean you don't get to grieve what it cost. Both can be true. Naming the loss doesn't make you ungrateful; it makes you honest, and honesty is where the healing actually starts.

What helps

You can rebuild a life abroad that's genuinely yours. A few things make the real difference:

  • Build something that's just yours. Not to fill time — a real anchor: work, study, a project, a cause, a class you show up to every week. A life needs a reason of its own, not only a location.
  • Name the imbalance as a team. The invisible labor becomes lighter the moment it's seen. Ask for partnership and appreciation, not just gratitude for the opportunity.
  • Keep one thread of your old identity. Stay in touch with your field, your friends, the parts of you that existed before this move. You don't have to dissolve into the spouse of.
  • Give the sacrifice a timeline. "We'll try this for a defined stretch, then reassess together" is far kinder to you than an open-ended, unspoken ask.
  • Be patient with the rebuild. Making a life from scratch takes longer than a flight. You're not behind. You're in the middle of something genuinely hard.

You are not a footnote to someone else's move

The word trailing makes it sound like you're just following along behind. You're not. You're doing one of the hardest, least-credited jobs in the whole relocation — holding a family steady while quietly rebuilding yourself in the background. That deserves to be seen, most of all by you. Give yourself the same patience and respect you'd give anyone starting a whole life over. You've earned it.

Common questions

What is a trailing spouse?

A trailing spouse (or accompanying partner) is the person who relocates abroad for their partner's job or opportunity rather than their own. They often set aside their career, community, and identity, and take on much of the invisible work of settling the family in a new country.

Is it normal to resent your partner after moving abroad for their job?

Yes, it's very common. When one partner has built-in structure and purpose and the other is starting from scratch, resentment can build — especially when the imbalance goes unnamed. It doesn't mean the relationship is broken; it usually means something real needs to be spoken about openly.

How do I find myself again as a trailing spouse?

Build something that's genuinely your own (work, study, a project, a community), keep a thread of your pre-move identity, name the invisible labor with your partner, and be patient — rebuilding a life takes far longer than the move itself.

This article is educational and reflective — not therapy, diagnosis, or a crisis service. If the loss and loneliness of relocating feel overwhelming, please reach out to someone you trust or a licensed professional. You deserve real support of your own.
