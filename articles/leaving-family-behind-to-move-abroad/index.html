The inner side of moving abroad

The Quiet Grief of Leaving Family Behind to Move Abroad

By Stephanie Johnson, LICSW, a licensed clinical social worker and expat

There is a particular kind of goodbye that happens at an airport, or a front door, before you move abroad. The kind where you hold on a beat too long, memorize a face, and then make yourself turn and walk toward the life you chose. And then you carry that goodbye with you, quietly, for a long time after.

Almost no one prepares you for this part. There is endless advice about visas and shipping and finding an apartment, and almost nothing about the ache of leaving the people who raised you, or grew up beside you, on the other side of the world. If that ache is sitting in your chest right now, you are not being dramatic and you are not ungrateful. You are grieving something real.

A grief no one prepares you for

When you move abroad, everyone celebrates the leaving. There are going-away dinners and excited questions and "you are so brave." Very few people think to ask the quieter question: what are you leaving behind, and how does that feel?

So the grief goes underground. It is a loss with no funeral, no casserole, no card. And because nobody names it, you assume you are the only one feeling it, and that maybe you should not feel it at all, since you are the one who booked the ticket.

You are allowed to grieve a choice you made on purpose. Choosing to go does not sign away your right to miss what you left.

The guilt that comes with the goodbye

For a lot of people, the grief of leaving family arrives tangled up with guilt. Guilt about aging parents you are no longer down the road from. Guilt about the birthdays, the ordinary Sundays, the milestones you will now see through a phone screen. Guilt about being the one who left.

Here is the gentlest true thing I can offer about that guilt: it is very often love with nowhere to go. You feel guilty because you love them, not because you did something wrong. Guilt likes to pose as a verdict, telling you that you are a bad daughter or son or sibling for wanting your own life. It is usually not a verdict at all. It is the size of your caring, showing up as an ache.

And it is worth saying plainly: leaving is not the same as abandoning. You can love your family deeply and still go build a life somewhere else. Both of those are allowed to be true in the same heart, on the same day.

How to carry the grief of leaving family behind

You do not resolve this kind of grief so much as learn to hold it well. A few things genuinely help.

  1. Let yourself actually grieve. Name it as grief, out loud or on paper. Naming a loss gives it somewhere to live besides the back of your throat, and it stops you from mistaking a normal ache for a sign you chose wrong.
  2. Say goodbye consciously. If you can, do not let the leaving happen in a blur of logistics. Mark it. A last walk, a shared meal, a letter you leave behind. Rituals help the heart catch up to what the calendar already knows.
  3. Build a rhythm of connection. Grief gets heavier when it is formless. Give the missing a container: a standing call home, same day, same time, so connection is a rhythm you can count on rather than something you have to summon on the hard nights.
  4. Let guilt be information, not a sentence. When the guilt rises, try asking what it is protecting. Usually it points to a relationship you want to keep tending. Let it prompt a message or a call, not a verdict about your worth.
  5. Be present where you are. One of the kindest things you can do for the people you left is to actually live the life you left for. Guilt tempts you to keep one foot back home. Honoring the goodbye means letting yourself arrive.
  6. Let both be true. You can miss them terribly and be glad you came. You can ache for home and still be exactly where you are meant to be. Holding both is not confusion. It is the honest shape of this life.

Love travels further than you think

The distance is real, and so is the grief, and I am not going to tell you it disappears. But the love does not stay behind at the airport. It goes with you, and it keeps reaching back across every time zone, in the standing phone calls and the photos and the way you still know each other even from far away. You did not stop being their family the day you boarded the plane. You just began the harder, braver work of loving them across a distance, and that is its own kind of devotion. Be gentle with yourself. Missing them this much is only proof of how much there was to leave.

Common questions

How do you deal with the guilt of moving away from family?

Start by recognizing that the guilt is usually love, not evidence of wrongdoing. Let it prompt connection, a call, a visit home, a standing check-in, rather than treating it as a verdict about your character. You are allowed to build your own life and still be a loving daughter, son, or sibling from far away.

Is it normal to grieve after moving abroad even though I chose it?

Completely. Choosing a move does not cancel the grief of what you left. It is one of the most common and least talked about parts of living abroad, precisely because everyone celebrates the leaving and few people ask about the loss underneath it.

How do you cope with leaving your family behind?

Grieve it honestly, say your goodbyes consciously, and set up a reliable rhythm of staying in touch so the connection does not depend on willpower. Then let yourself be present in your new life. Missing them and living fully where you are can, and should, coexist.

This article is educational and reflective, not therapy, diagnosis, or a crisis service. If you are struggling beyond what a journal can hold, please reach out to someone you trust, a licensed professional, or local emergency services. You deserve real support.
