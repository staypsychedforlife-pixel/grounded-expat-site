The inner side of moving abroad

When Moving Abroad Strains Your Marriage

By Stephanie Johnson, LICSW — licensed clinical social worker & expat

Moving abroad is supposed to be the adventure you do together — the shared leap, the story you'll tell for years. So it can be quietly disorienting when the move that was meant to bring you closer starts pulling at the seams of your marriage instead. If your relationship has felt harder since the relocation, you're not doing it wrong, and you're not alone. This is one of the most common — and least talked about — strains of expat life.

As a therapist who has also lived the transition, here's why relocation puts such specific pressure on a marriage, and what actually helps protect it.

The move becomes a third person in the room

Back home, your relationship was held up by a whole ecosystem — friends, family, routines, work, hobbies, a hundred small distractions. Abroad, especially in the early months, most of that scaffolding falls away, and the two of you become each other's entire world: only support, only social life, only sounding board. That's an enormous load for any couple to carry. Frictions that used to get diluted across a full life now land directly, and only, between you.

The invisible imbalance

Most moves happen for one person's job or opportunity. Which means one partner lands with built-in structure — colleagues, purpose, a reason to get up every day — and the other, the trailing spouse, lands with none of it. One of you is busy and needed; the other is untethered, often doing the invisible labor of building a home in a foreign place while grieving the identity, career, and community they left behind. This asymmetry, more than any single argument, is what quietly corrodes a lot of expat marriages.

The strain usually isn't a failure of love. It's the weight of an imbalance neither of you chose, sitting unnamed between you.

The resentment no one says out loud

Here's the trap. The trailing partner often feels guilty for struggling — this was a wonderful opportunity, I should be grateful — so the resentment goes underground instead of getting spoken. The working partner feels guilty for uprooting their spouse, or defensive about it, so they avoid the subject too. You both love each other. You both desperately don't want the move to be the thing that breaks you. So you say nothing — and the silence does far more damage than the honesty ever would have.

Culture shock spills into the relationship

When you're dysregulated — jet-lagged, lonely, cognitively exhausted by a place where nothing is automatic yet — you have less patience, a shorter fuse, less generosity to give. A lot of what looks like a marriage problem in the first months is really two nervous systems in culture shock, taking it out on the nearest safe person. Naming that, in the moment, can lower the temperature fast: this is the move talking, not us.

What actually helps

You don't need to have handled this perfectly to turn it around. A few things make a real difference:

  • Name the imbalance out loud. You can't repair what you're both pretending isn't there. Simply saying "one of us has a whole life here and one of us is building one from scratch, and that's genuinely hard" can be a release valve.
  • Give the trailing partner something of their own. Not just a hobby — a real reason to be there: work, a class, a project, a community. A marriage does far better when both people have a life, not just a location.
  • Make any sacrifice time-bound, not open-ended. "We'll try this for a defined stretch, then decide together" protects you both. An unspoken, indefinite ask is what slowly hardens into resentment.
  • Don't make big decisions while dysregulated. The month-three low is not the moment to decide whether the marriage or the move is failing. Get steady first; decide later.
  • Protect a little of the old "us." Keep one ritual that belongs only to the two of you, unrelated to the stress of settling in. It reminds you that you're still a couple, not just co-managers of a hard relocation.

The move isn't the enemy

The strain you're feeling doesn't mean you made the wrong choice, or married the wrong person. It means you're carrying something genuinely heavy, mostly in the dark. Bring it into the light — name the imbalance, share the invisible load, keep talking even on the nights it would be easier not to — and the very move that strained your marriage can become the thing that ends up deepening it. Plenty of couples come out the other side closer than they went in. Not because it was easy. Because they faced it together.

Common questions

Why is moving abroad so hard on a marriage?

Relocation strips away your usual support systems, so the two of you become each other's whole world, and it usually creates an imbalance — one partner has built-in structure and purpose, the other is starting from scratch. That asymmetry, plus culture shock and homesickness, puts a specific strain on the relationship.

Is it normal for a relationship to struggle after moving abroad?

Very. Relationship strain is one of the most common and least discussed parts of expat life. It usually doesn't mean the relationship is failing — it means you're both under real pressure that hasn't been named yet.

What is a trailing spouse, and why is it hard?

A trailing spouse, or accompanying partner, is the person who moves for their partner's job or opportunity rather than their own. It's hard because they often set aside their career, community, and identity, and arrive with none of the built-in structure the working partner has — while doing much of the invisible work of settling the family.

This article is educational and reflective — not therapy, couples counseling, diagnosis, or a crisis service. If your relationship is in serious distress, or you don't feel safe, please reach out to a licensed couples therapist or a trusted professional. You both deserve real support.
