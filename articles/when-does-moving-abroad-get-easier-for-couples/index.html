When Does Moving Abroad Get Easier for Couples?

By Stephanie Johnson, LICSW — licensed clinical social worker & expat

In the thick of it — both of you exhausted, snapping over nothing, quietly wondering what this move did to your relationship — one question matters more than any other: does this get easier? As a therapist who's also lived the move, the honest answer is yes, it usually does. But not on the timeline you might expect, and not entirely on its own.

Here's what the arc actually looks like for a couple, and what moves it from barely surviving to something that feels like the two of you again.

Why it's hardest at the start

The early months are the hardest for a couple, and there's a reason. You're both dysregulated — jet-lagged, overloaded, homesick — so you each have less patience at the exact moment you need more. Your whole support system has shrunk down to each other, so every friction lands directly between you. And the imbalance of the move — one of you with a built-in life, the other starting from scratch — is at its rawest before either of you has named it. That's a great deal of pressure on one relationship.

The turning point isn't a date on the calendar

People wait for a magic month when it will suddenly click. But for couples, "easier" rarely arrives just because time passed. It arrives when a few things change: when you name the imbalance out loud instead of resenting it in silence, when the accompanying partner starts building a life of their own, and when you both stop white-knuckling and start talking honestly about how hard it's been. Time helps — but the conversations are what actually turn it.

For a couple, it doesn't get easier when enough time passes. It gets easier when you stop carrying it in silence.

What "easier" actually looks like

Easier doesn't mean conflict-free, or back to exactly how it was. It means you've built a shared language for the hard parts — you can say "this is the move talking, not us," and you both know what it means. It means the invisible labor is seen, the imbalance is being actively worked on, and you're a team facing the challenge instead of two people quietly blaming each other for it. Plenty of couples come out the far side closer than they went in.

What speeds it up

  • Both of you have a life, not just a location. When the trailing partner has their own anchor, the whole relationship exhales.
  • Name the imbalance — together and early. It gets lighter the moment it's spoken.
  • Don't make big decisions while dysregulated. The month-three low is not the time to decide the marriage or the move is failing.
  • Protect a little of the old "us." Keep one ritual that's just the two of you, unrelated to the stress of settling in.
  • Talk on purpose. Don't wait for a blow-up to check in on how you're each really doing.

When it's more than adjustment

Most of the strain of moving abroad eases with time and honesty. But if what's between you has curdled into contempt, if the conversations feel impossible, or if you don't feel safe — that isn't something to grit through. Please reach out to a couples therapist or a trusted professional. Getting help early isn't a sign the relationship is failing; it's how good ones protect themselves.

It gets easier — usually because you make it

So yes: for most couples, moving abroad gets easier. The novelty settles, the trailing partner finds their footing, and the two of you find your rhythm again. But the couples for whom it turns fastest aren't the ones who simply waited it out. They're the ones who named the hard thing, shared the load, and kept talking on the nights it would have been easier to go quiet. Do that, and the move that strained you can become the very thing that proves how much you can carry together.

Common questions

How long does it take for a couple to adjust after moving abroad?

It varies, but the hardest stretch is usually the first few months, easing over the following year as the novelty settles and the accompanying partner finds their footing. For couples, though, "easier" comes less from time passing and more from naming the imbalance and talking honestly.

Is it normal for a relationship to struggle after relocating?

Very. Relationship strain is one of the most common parts of expat life, and it usually doesn't mean the relationship is failing — it means you're both under real, unnamed pressure.

Will our marriage survive moving abroad?

Most do, and many come out stronger — especially couples who name the imbalance, make sure both people have a life of their own, and keep communicating. If there's contempt or you don't feel safe, please reach out to a couples therapist; getting help early is how strong relationships protect themselves.

This article is educational and reflective — not couples therapy, diagnosis, or a crisis service. If your relationship is in serious distress, or you don't feel safe, please reach out to a licensed couples therapist or a trusted professional. You both deserve real support.
