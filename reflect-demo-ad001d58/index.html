Finding Your People Abroad

The brave, ordinary work of building a life full of people.
Created by Stephanie Johnson, LICSW

One of the hardest and least-talked-about parts of moving abroad is starting your whole social world over from zero. The friendships that knew you — the ones where you didn’t have to explain yourself — are far away, and building new ones as an adult, in a new culture, can feel slow and exposing. If you’re lonely, you are not doing this wrong. You’re doing something genuinely hard.

This workbook helps you name the loneliness honestly, loosen the stories that keep you stuck, and take small, brave steps toward real connection. Work through it in order or dip in where you need it. Part One

Name It

Telling the truth about the loneliness

The Truth About Loneliness Abroad

Loneliness abroad isn’t proof that something’s wrong with you — it’s the natural result of leaving a whole web of relationships behind and not having rebuilt one yet. It’s situational, not a character flaw. Let’s start by telling the truth about it.

A note for you

Feeling lonely doesn’t mean you’re unlikable or bad at friendship. It means you’re between communities — and that’s a place, not a verdict.

SFrom my experience

About two and a half months after landing in Thailand, the excitement had faded. The vibrant colors, bustling streets, and sense of adventure that once felt exhilarating had become the backdrop to a quieter realization: nothing felt familiar anymore. I didn’t have a kitchen where I could make the meals that once grounded me, and even the small comforts of daily routine had disappeared. My partner was the only familiar person in my world, yet I found myself feeling distant from him too. It was in that moment I realized that homesickness isn’t always about missing a place — sometimes it’s about missing the version of yourself that knew where you belonged.

— Stephanie

What You Left Behind

Before you can build new friendships, it helps to honor the ones you moved away from. The people who knew your history, your jokes, your context — losing daily access to them is a real grief, even when the move was your choice. Naming it makes room for the new.

A note for you

Missing your old friends isn’t disloyalty to your new life. Grief and hope can share the same heart.

Who are the friendships you miss most — and what specifically did they give you that you haven’t found here yet?

The Stories in the Way

Loneliness comes with a script, and the script keeps us stuck: ‘Everyone already has their people.’ ‘I’m too old / too awkward / too busy.’ ‘If they wanted to be my friend, they’d reach out.’ These feel like facts. They’re usually fear. Let’s get them into the light.

A note for you

The belief that it’s too late, or that you’re the only one struggling, is the loneliness talking — not the truth. Most people around you are quietly hoping someone will make the first move.

Is there one story that plays louder than the rest — like ‘everyone already has their people’ or ‘it’s too late for me’?
Let’s bring that one into the light.

Which of those was the most uncomfortable to write down? That’s usually the one with the most grip on you.

Part Two

Build It

The brave, repeated work of connection

Where Your People Actually Are

Adult friendships rarely appear out of nowhere — they grow from proximity plus repetition. The same faces, the same rooms, again and again, until strangers become familiar and familiar becomes friend. This is about finding those repeated rooms on purpose.

A note for you

You don’t have to be charming or lucky. You have to be somewhere, more than once. Friendship is mostly repetition — and that you can plan.

What are you genuinely interested in — hobbies, causes, sport, faith, creative things — that gather other people regularly? Don’t filter for ‘realistic’ yet.
Is there already a place you show up to regularly — a class, a café, a group, a gym, the school gate?
Good — that’s fertile ground. Let’s actually use it.
Where is it, and whose faces are starting to feel familiar there — even ones you haven’t spoken to?
What’s one small, low-stakes thing you could say to a familiar face next time — a comment, a question, a hello by name? one small thing
Then the first move is finding one — proximity plus repetition is how adult friendship actually forms.
List every repeated context you could plausibly show up to more than once: a class, a run club, a co-working space, a place of worship, a language exchange, the same café. exercise
Which one feels least scary to try first? What’s the very next step to get yourself there this week? one small thing

The Brave First Move

At some point, connection asks something of us: to say hello, to suggest a coffee, to be the one who reaches out first. It feels exposing because it is — but it’s also the single most powerful thing you can do. Most people are relieved when someone else goes first.

A note for you

Being the one who initiates isn’t desperate or ‘too much.’ It’s generous. You’re giving someone the very thing they were also hoping for.

When it comes to making the first move — inviting someone, following up — what actually stops you? Name the real fear underneath.

From Acquaintance to Friend

The move from friendly-acquaintance to real friend happens through consistency and a little vulnerability — showing up again, remembering details, letting yourself be slightly more known each time. It’s slower than we’d like, and it’s mostly repetition, not chemistry.

A note for you

You don’t have to force closeness or rush it. Real friendship is built in small, repeated moments of showing up — and you’re allowed to let it take the time it takes.

SFrom my experience

I thought making friends abroad would happen naturally, but it took much longer than I expected. For months, I kept showing up — to the same places, the same events — even when I felt awkward or like I didn’t quite fit in. Most interactions were brief, and I often wondered if I was doing something wrong. Then, little by little, things started to change. Conversations lasted longer, familiar faces became people who genuinely smiled when they saw me, and I realized I no longer felt like a complete outsider. It wasn’t one big moment that created a sense of belonging — it was the accumulation of many small moments and the willingness to keep showing up, even before I felt connected.

— Stephanie

Your Connection Plan

Loneliness eases not with one grand solution but with a handful of small, repeated commitments. This last page gathers what you’ve learned into a gentle, doable plan — one you can actually keep.

A note for you

You don’t have to build a whole community this month. You have to take one brave, ordinary step toward people — and then another. That’s how everyone’s people are found.
